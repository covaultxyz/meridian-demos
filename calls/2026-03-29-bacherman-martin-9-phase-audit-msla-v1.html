PHASE 4: BLIND SPOT ANALYSIS

Blind Spot 1: The Agreement Assumes Meridian Has Bargaining Power

**What is not being seen**: The MSLA is drafted from a position of significant leverage — "Meridian owns everything, Client buys access." This assumes the Client needs Meridian more than Meridian needs the Client. For a startup or early-stage company, the first few clients may have more leverage than the agreement assumes. A sophisticated Client could reject the entire framework and insist on a more balanced structure.

**Why the author's position makes it invisible**: The document was drafted in reaction to v3-v6, where Meridian was giving away too much. The pendulum may have swung too far in the other direction. The author is optimizing for Meridian's protection, not for deal-closing efficiency.

**Damage if unaddressed**: If the Client's attorney reads this as a one-sided power grab, the entire framework may be rejected, losing the structural improvements (tiered licensing, SOW discipline, continuous access) that are genuinely bilateral. The "Why This Structure Is Different" section at the end explicitly criticizes the Client's previous position, which could be adversarial if the Client reads it.

Blind Spot 2: The Tier System Has No Pricing, Making It Aspirational

**What is not being seen**: The 4-tier license system is the core commercial innovation, but it has no price signal. Without knowing what each tier costs, a Client cannot evaluate the offer. The menu has dishes described but no prices listed. This makes the tier system a structural framework, not a commercial tool.

**Why the author's position makes it invisible**: The author structured the tiers conceptually (what rights come with each level) but deferred the pricing to SOW negotiation. This makes the agreement intellectually complete but commercially incomplete.

**Damage if unaddressed**: The first Client conversation will immediately hit "what does Tier 2 cost?" — and the agreement provides no answer. This weakens Meridian's negotiating position by making it seem like the tiers were designed to protect IP rather than to sell products.

Blind Spot 3: The Agreement Does Not Acknowledge Meridian's Obligations to the Client

**What is not being seen**: The document is almost entirely about what Meridian keeps and what the Client cannot do. The provisions that define what Meridian must deliver (Section 5, Section 7.1, Section 9.3-9.4) exist but are relatively thin compared to the IP retention sections. A Client reading this would feel the agreement is about Meridian's rights, not their obligations.

**Why the author's position makes it invisible**: The document was designed to fix the IP giveaway problem. Delivery obligations were carried over from v5 but given less emphasis in the new framing.

**Damage if unaddressed**: A Client's attorney will highlight the imbalance. Standard commercial agreements balance rights with obligations. The agreement needs more specificity on what Meridian commits to deliver, how quality is measured, and what happens if Meridian fails to perform.

Blind Spot 4: The "WHY THIS STRUCTURE IS DIFFERENT" Section Is Internal Strategy, Not Client-Facing

**What is not being seen**: The final section of the agreement compares the old model to the new model, explicitly calling out the previous v3-v6 framework as "starting from the wrong premise." This is internal strategy commentary that should not be in a client-facing agreement.

**Why the author's position makes it invisible**: It was written during the frame-shift conversation as an explanation for Meridian's team. It was included in the draft to document the rationale. But it reads as adversarial to a Client who just saw their preferred structure described as "the wrong starting position."

**Damage if unaddressed**: Client's counsel will use this section to argue Meridian is negotiating in bad faith or has reversed its prior position. Remove it from the client-facing version; keep it as an internal brief.

Blind Spot 5: Puerto Rico Enforceability of "Irrevocable" Licenses Conditioned on Payment

**What is not being seen**: Section 3.2 says licenses are "irrevocable (except for material breach)" but also "subject to payment" — Meridian may suspend upon 30-day uncured nonpayment. In PR contract law, a license that is both "irrevocable" and "suspendable for nonpayment" may be interpreted as a conditional license, not truly irrevocable. The PR Civil Code (Art. 1210, good faith) may require a court to choose one characterization.

**Why the author's position makes it invisible**: Both terms are standard in US software licensing. But PR operates under a mixed common/civil law system where contract interpretation follows the intent-of-the-parties doctrine (Art. 1233 CC PR), and courts may scrutinize apparent contradictions more aggressively than common-law courts.

**Damage if unaddressed**: A Client in payment dispute could argue the license was never truly irrevocable, undermining the perpetual license promise. Resolution: clarify that the license is irrevocable once all applicable fees are paid in full, and that the suspension mechanism applies only during the cure period for unpaid fees, after which the license becomes fully vested and irrevocable.
