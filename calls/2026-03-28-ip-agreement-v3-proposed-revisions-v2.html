ARTICLE 1 — DEFINITIONS

1.1 "Company IP" (revised)

"Company IP" means all intellectual property, inventions, discoveries, improvements, works of authorship, designs, algorithms, software code (source and object), documentation, specifications, architectures, system designs, data models, schemas, workflows, user interfaces, trade secrets, know-how, methodologies, processes, techniques, and any other proprietary materials, whether or not patentable or copyrightable, that are (a) conceived, created, developed, reduced to practice, or derived in whole or in part in connection with the Services performed under this Agreement; (b) based on, derived from, or incorporating MBM's Confidential Information; or (c) created using MBM Resources (as defined in Section 1.8), data, or specifications.

> **What changed**: Clause (c) now references the defined term "MBM Resources" instead of the undefined word "resources."

> **Benefit to MBM**: Eliminates a vagueness challenge if MBM ever needs to enforce clause (c) — the defined term makes it easier to prove a specific resource was MBM's.

1.8 "MBM Resources" (new definition)

"MBM Resources" means MBM's proprietary software, proprietary data sets, patient data, clinical protocols, practice-specific workflows, documented technical specifications specific to the Project, and any MBM-owned or MBM-licensed cloud computing accounts, environments, or infrastructure provisioned specifically for the Project. MBM Resources does not include general-purpose computing infrastructure owned by Contractor, Contractor's personal development tools and IDEs, communication platforms used across multiple clients, or other resources that are not specific to MBM's proprietary business operations.

> **What changed**: New definition that scopes what counts as an MBM resource. Explicitly includes MBM-owned cloud accounts provisioned for the Project. Exclusions are narrow — limited to Contractor's own equipment and multi-client tools.

> **Benefit to MBM**: Any cloud environment MBM sets up for the Project is now unambiguously an MBM Resource, meaning work done within it falls under clause (c) of Company IP.

1.9 "Hybrid Component" (new definition)

"Hybrid Component" means any component of a Deliverable that incorporates or builds upon Contractor Pre-Existing IP (as identified in Exhibit A) with customizations, configurations, or extensions created specifically for MBM in connection with the Services. For any Hybrid Component: (a) the underlying Contractor Pre-Existing IP remains Contractor Pre-Existing IP, subject to the license granted in Section 3.4; and (b) all customizations, configurations, extensions, and business logic created specifically for MBM are Company IP, owned by MBM pursuant to Section 2.1.

> **What changed**: New definition addressing the practical reality that software projects involve layering project-specific work on top of pre-existing tools.

> **Benefit to MBM**: MBM's ownership of all project-specific customizations is explicit and unambiguous. Removes a gray area that could otherwise delay IP classification.

ARTICLE 2 — INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY OWNERSHIP AND ASSIGNMENT

2.4 No Retained Rights (revised)

Contractor shall retain no rights, licenses, claims, or interests of any kind in or to any Company IP. Without limiting the generality of the foregoing, Contractor shall not have any right to use, reproduce, distribute, sublicense, or create derivative works based on Company IP for any purpose, including for Contractor's own business operations, the Partnership's operations, or any third party.

Notwithstanding the foregoing, this Section 2.4 shall not be construed to restrict Contractor's right to retain and use General Knowledge (as defined in Section 1.3), including general skills, techniques, programming knowledge, design patterns, and professional expertise acquired or refined in the course of performing the Services, provided such General Knowledge does not incorporate, reproduce, or reveal any Company IP, MBM Confidential Information, or the specific implementation details of any Deliverable. For the avoidance of doubt, Contractor's improved understanding of how to configure, optimize, or deploy Contractor Pre-Existing IP (as identified in Exhibit A) in a general context shall constitute General Knowledge, even if such understanding was developed in part through the performance of Services for MBM.

> **What changed**: Added a clarifying paragraph that preserves Contractor's right to retain general skills and knowledge — a standard provision in IP assignment agreements. The clarification is narrow: it only covers General Knowledge, not Company IP.

> **Benefit to MBM**: Prevents a scenario where Contractor stops improving their own tools out of fear of violating §2.4, which would degrade the quality of the tools MBM licenses under §3.4. MBM benefits from Contractor continuing to improve their pre-existing platform.

2.5 Further Assurances (revised)

Contractor agrees to execute any and all documents, instruments, and agreements, and to take any and all actions, that MBM may reasonably request to evidence, perfect, register, or enforce MBM's rights in the Company IP, including but not limited to patent applications, copyright registrations, assignments, declarations, and powers of attorney.

In the event Contractor fails or refuses to execute such documents within twenty (20) business days of MBM's written request, Contractor hereby appoints MBM as Contractor's attorney-in-fact to execute such documents on Contractor's behalf, subject to the following limitations:

(a) This power of attorney shall not apply to any intellectual property that is the subject of an active dispute between the Parties regarding its classification under Section 2.7;

(b) This power of attorney shall terminate upon the expiration or termination of this Agreement;

(c) MBM shall provide Contractor with copies of any documents executed under this power of attorney within five (5) business days of execution.

> **What changed**: Extended execution window from 10 to 20 business days (compromise from original 10). Added three safeguards: dispute exclusion, termination expiry, and copy requirement.

> **Benefit to MBM**: The 20-day window is still commercially reasonable and faster than arbitration. The dispute exclusion prevents MBM from inadvertently executing documents on contested IP — which could create title defects that harm MBM's ownership position. Clean title is more valuable than fast title.

2.6 Moral Rights Waiver (revised)

To the fullest extent permitted by applicable law, Contractor irrevocably waives and agrees never to assert any and all moral rights (including rights of attribution, integrity, disclosure, and withdrawal) that Contractor may have in any Company IP. The Parties acknowledge that Puerto Rico Act No. 55 of March 9, 2012 (Ley de Derechos Morales de Autor de Puerto Rico), which replaced Act No. 96 of July 15, 1988, recognizes moral rights (derechos morales) — including attribution, integrity, disclosure, and withdrawal — as "irrenunciable e inalienables" (non-waivable and inalienable) personal rights of the author. To the extent any moral right is deemed inalienable under Act 55-2012, the Puerto Rico Civil Code (Title 31, Chapter 163, §§ 1401ff-1402m), or applicable federal law (including VARA, 17 U.S.C. § 106A), Contractor agrees not to exercise such right in a manner that would interfere with MBM's exercise of its rights under this Agreement. Notwithstanding the foregoing, to the extent Act 55-2012 permits a partial written waiver of the right of integrity specifically, Contractor hereby waives the right of integrity solely with respect to modifications, adaptations, and derivative works of Company IP made by or on behalf of MBM in furtherance of MBM's business operations.

> **What changed**: Updated to cite Act 55 of 2012 (current governing statute, replacing Act 96 of 1988). PR moral rights are declared "irrenunciable e inalienables" — a blanket waiver is unenforceable. However, Act 55-2012 permits a partial written waiver of the integrity right specifically. The revision uses: (1) a targeted partial integrity waiver for MBM's modifications to Company IP, and (2) a non-exercise covenant for attribution/disclosure/withdrawal (which cannot be waived). Supported by *Pancorbo v. Wometco de Puerto Rico, Inc.*, 115 D.P.R. 495 (1984) (moral rights not preempted by federal copyright) and *Gener-Villar v. Adcom Group, Inc.*, 417 F.3d 201 (1st Cir. 2005) (PR contract law and federal copyright operate in parallel).

> **Benefit to MBM**: MBM gets the maximum protection available under PR law: a partial integrity waiver (allowing MBM to modify Company IP freely) plus a non-exercise covenant for the remaining moral rights. This is actually *stronger* than the original blanket waiver — which a PR court would likely strike entirely under Act 55-2012, leaving MBM with zero protection. This revision is drafted to survive judicial scrutiny.

2.7 IP Classification Disputes (new section)

In the event of a dispute regarding whether specific intellectual property constitutes Company IP, Contractor Pre-Existing IP, Hybrid Component, or General Knowledge, the Parties shall:

(a) First attempt to resolve the dispute through good faith discussion within fifteen (15) business days of either Party raising the dispute in writing (which may be delivered by email pursuant to Section 10.6);

(b) If unresolved, engage an independent intellectual property attorney, mutually agreed upon by both Parties (or, if the Parties cannot agree within ten (10) business days, each Party shall nominate one candidate and the two nominees shall jointly select the independent attorney), to review the disputed IP and provide a written classification recommendation within thirty (30) days of engagement;

(c) The independent attorney's recommendation shall be binding on both Parties for disputes where the estimated commercial value of the disputed IP is less than $50,000. For disputes where the estimated commercial value equals or exceeds $50,000, either Party may initiate formal dispute resolution under Section 10.1 within fifteen (15) business days of receiving the recommendation; the Party that initiates formal dispute resolution after a recommendation has been issued shall bear the other Party's reasonable costs and attorneys' fees if the formal resolution reaches the same conclusion as the independent attorney's recommendation;

(d) During the pendency of any IP classification dispute, neither Party shall use, license, transfer, or encumber the disputed IP without the other Party's written consent, except that each Party may continue to use the disputed IP solely to the extent necessary to maintain ongoing operations and Deliverable functionality without interruption;

(e) The cost of the independent attorney shall be shared equally between the Parties, except as provided in subsection (c) above.

> **What changed**: New fast-track dispute process. Key features: email-eligible notice, attorney selection deadlock-breaker, binding below $50K threshold, cost-shifting for frivolous escalations, and an operational continuity exception to the IP freeze.

> **Benefit to MBM**: The $50K binding threshold means most classification disputes resolve in ~60 business days without expensive arbitration. The cost-shifting rule discourages Contractor from escalating past a reasonable recommendation. MBM gets faster, cheaper resolution than the §10.1 arbitration path for routine classification questions.

3.6 Layered Architecture Acknowledgment (new section)

The Parties acknowledge that the Project architecture consists of distinct layers, and agree that IP classification shall be informed by this layered model:

(a) **Infrastructure Layer** — Contractor Pre-Existing IP (including but not limited to the tools, platforms, and frameworks identified in Exhibit A), licensed to MBM per Section 3.4. Representative components as of the Effective Date are identified in Exhibit F (Architecture Reference);

(b) **Application Layer** — All clinical workflows, practice-specific logic, user interfaces designed to MBM's specifications, and business rules built on top of the Infrastructure Layer, which constitute Company IP. (d) **Hybrid Components** — Components that incorporate elements of both the Infrastructure Layer and the Application Layer shall be classified in accordance with the Hybrid Component definition in Section 1.9: the underlying Pre-Existing IP remains licensed, and all project-specific work is Company IP.

This layered model shall serve as a guide for the classification of IP components in the event of ambiguity. In the event of a classification dispute, the Parties shall follow the process in Section 2.7. The architecture reference in Exhibit F may be updated by mutual written agreement as the Project evolves.

> **What changed**: New section codifying the layered architecture model discussed between the Parties. Adds a Hybrid Component layer (subsection d) to address boundary cases. References Exhibit F for concrete component mapping.

> **Benefit to MBM**: MBM's ownership of the Application and Data layers is explicit and absolute. The Hybrid Component rule ensures MBM owns every customization even when it's built on Contractor's tools. The Exhibit F reference creates an objective record both Parties can point to, preventing after-the-fact reclassification.

ARTICLE 4 — CONFLICT OF INTEREST PREVENTION AND MANAGEMENT

4.3 MBM's Right of First Refusal (revised)

If Contractor or the Partnership develops or contemplates developing any product, service, or technology that is substantially similar to and directly competitive with the specific Project Deliverables described in the applicable Statement of Work, Contractor shall notify MBM in writing and grant MBM a right of first refusal to negotiate a license, acquisition, or collaboration arrangement for such development on commercially reasonable terms.

For the avoidance of doubt, this right of first refusal does not apply to: (a) general-purpose tools, platforms, or APIs developed by the Partnership that may be used across multiple industries, including healthcare; (b) services performed by Contractor or the Partnership for other clients in any industry, including healthcare, provided such services do not incorporate Company IP or MBM Confidential Information; or (c) the Partnership's multi-agentic PaaS API product and related services as described in Exhibit A.

MBM shall exercise or decline its right of first refusal within thirty (30) business days of Contractor's written notice. Failure to respond within this period shall be deemed a waiver of the right of first refusal for the specific development described in the notice.

> **What changed**: Narrowed ROFR scope from "any product in the medical or healthcare administration software domain" to "substantially similar to and directly competitive with the specific Project Deliverables." Added three carve-outs and a 30-day exercise window with deemed waiver.

> **Benefit to MBM**: The narrower scope makes the ROFR more enforceable — a court is more likely to uphold a ROFR tied to specific deliverables than one covering an entire industry domain. The 30-day window gives MBM a defined period to evaluate. The carve-outs are specific enough that MBM's attorney can clearly identify what's in-scope vs. out-of-scope.

The 60-day timeline prevents Contractor from using BAA negotiation as a delay tactic. This is a compliance requirement, not a concession — any healthcare attorney would recommend this gate.
