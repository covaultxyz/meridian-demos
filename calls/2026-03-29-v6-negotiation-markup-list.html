Structural Thesis

V5 is well-drafted and structurally sound. But it contains a dependency trap: MBM owns the Application Layer and Data Layer absolutely, while remaining commercially and technically dependent on a broad Infrastructure Layer that the Contractor controls. The "shall govern" language in Section 3.6 — which v5 strengthened at Bacherman Martin's recommendation — now equally hardens the Contractor's retained IP position.

**The v6 redlines solve this by ensuring MBM has practical control, not just legal ownership.** Specifically: broad license rights for embedded tooling, module-level specificity in Exhibit A, SOW-conditioned assignment, express acceptance for major deliverables, extended warranty, continuous source code access, and narrowed contractor exit rights.

MF-1: Section 2.1 — SOW as Condition Precedent to Assignment

**V5 Current**: Section 2.1 is listed as "not revised" (unchanged from v3). The absolute assignment operates unconditionally — Contractor assigns all Company IP upon creation, regardless of whether a detailed SOW exists.

**Problem**: MBM commits to ownership mechanics before scope, deliverables, milestones, and payment are locked down. Assignment without a commercial bargain creates a leverage asymmetry.

**Proposed Addition** (new subsection 2.1A):

> **2.1A Condition Precedent.** Notwithstanding Section 2.1, the assignment of Company IP under this Agreement shall apply only to work product conceived, created, or developed in the performance of Services described in an executed Statement of Work (Exhibit D). No Services shall commence, and no use of MBM Resources shall be authorized, until the applicable Statement of Work has been executed by both Parties. For the avoidance of doubt, any work product created outside the scope of an executed Statement of Work shall not constitute Company IP and shall not be subject to the assignment in Section 2.1, unless such work product was created using MBM Resources or MBM Confidential Information (in which case Sections 1.1(b) and 1.1(c) apply regardless).

**Annotation**:

> **Benefit to MBM**: Assignment is now tied to a defined commercial bargain. MBM does not receive IP without paying for it under a negotiated SOW. This strengthens MBM's title — clean chain from SOW → Services → Deliverables → Assignment.

> **Benefit to Contractor**: Work outside executed SOWs is not captured. Contractor's independent work remains independent. The MBM Resources / Confidential Information triggers are preserved — if Contractor uses MBM's stuff, it's still Company IP regardless.

> **Trade-off**: MBM loses the ability to claim IP created in informal or pre-SOW work. This is the correct trade — title based on a clear SOW is more defensible in diligence, financing, and enforcement than title based on an undefined scope.

MF-2: Section 3.4(b) — Expanded License Rights for Embedded Pre-Existing IP

**V5 Current**: "MBM is granted a perpetual, irrevocable, royalty-free, non-exclusive license to use any such tools solely to the extent embedded in or necessary for the operation, maintenance, and modification of the Deliverables, but not for the development of new, standalone products or services unrelated to the Project."

**Problem**: "Operation, maintenance, and modification" is too narrow. MBM may need to host, copy, migrate, security-test, transition to a replacement vendor, use in adjacent internal systems, or allow an acquirer/affiliate to operate the Deliverables — all of which arguably fall outside "operation, maintenance, and modification."

**Proposed Replacement for Section 3.4(b)**:

> (b) MBM is granted a perpetual, irrevocable, royalty-free, non-exclusive, worldwide license to use, host, copy, execute, display, modify, maintain, test (including security and penetration testing), adapt, create derivative works of, and sublicense any such tools, solely to the extent embedded in, integrated with, or necessary for: (i) the operation, maintenance, modification, enhancement, and scaling of the Deliverables; (ii) migration of the Deliverables to replacement infrastructure, platforms, or vendors; (iii) security review, vulnerability assessment, and compliance auditing of the Deliverables; (iv) internal use by MBM's affiliates, successors, and assigns in connection with the Deliverables; and (v) engagement of third-party contractors or service providers to perform any of the foregoing on MBM's behalf. This license shall not extend to the development of new, standalone products or services that do not incorporate or build upon the Deliverables.

**Annotation**:

> **Benefit to MBM**: MBM can now migrate, security-test, bring in replacement vendors, allow an acquirer to operate, and sublicense embedded tooling — all without going back to Contractor for permission. This is what "practical control" means.

> **Benefit to Contractor**: The license is still limited to the Deliverables context. Contractor's tools cannot be used for standalone products unrelated to the Deliverables. The Contractor's right to use these tools for other clients is unaffected.

> **Why this matters**: Without this expansion, MBM "owns" the application layer but cannot practically operate it without Contractor's ongoing cooperation for any infrastructure change. That is vendor lock-in, not ownership.

MF-3: Section 2.7 — Strengthen Presumption, Limit Dispute Scope

**V5 Current**: Section 2.7 creates a formal IP classification dispute process. During disputes, neither party can use, license, transfer, or encumber the disputed IP (with an operational continuity exception).

**Problem**: The dispute mechanism creates title uncertainty. In diligence, financing, or enforcement, any formally disputed IP creates a cloud on title.

**Proposed Additions to Section 2.7**:

Add new subsection (f):

> (f) **Presumption of Company IP.** All work product conceived, created, or developed in connection with Services performed under an executed Statement of Work shall be presumed to constitute Company IP unless (i) the specific item was identified in Exhibit A prior to its first use in connection with the Project, with module-level specificity as required by Exhibit A, and (ii) the item existed in substantially complete form prior to the Effective Date or was independently developed by Contractor outside the scope of Services and without use of MBM Resources or MBM Confidential Information. The burden of proof for rebutting this presumption shall rest with the Party claiming the item is not Company IP.

Add new subsection (g):

> (g) **Title Preservation During Disputes.** Notwithstanding subsection (d), MBM's rights in Company IP (including the right to represent ownership in financing, diligence, licensing, and enforcement proceedings) shall not be affected by the pendency of any IP classification dispute, except with respect to the specific items expressly identified in the dispute notice. A pending dispute regarding one component shall not cloud MBM's title to any other Company IP.

**Annotation**:

> **Benefit to MBM**: Burden of proof is now on the party claiming something is NOT Company IP. MBM's title to non-disputed IP is explicitly preserved during any dispute. This eliminates the "title cloud" risk in diligence and financing.

> **Benefit to Contractor**: The presumption is rebuttable with clear evidence (prior existence or independent development). The dispute process itself is unchanged.

> **Trade-off**: The Contractor's ability to contest IP classification is preserved but the starting position is now that everything is Company IP. This is the standard employer/client position in IP assignment agreements.

MF-4: Exhibit A — Module-Level Specificity Required

**V5 Current**: Exhibit A lists 8 items with broad category labels: "MERIDIAN Daemon Mesh," "Memory Intelligence Graph," "Reusable Code Libraries," etc. Descriptions reference entire systems, not specific modules or functions.

**Problem**: Broad labels can capture more than intended. "MERIDIAN Daemon Mesh — Distributed AI daemon system including orchestrator, brain, mesh, and collaboration layers; NATS messaging infrastructure; pulse sentinel system; blue/green deployment system" is an entire platform, not a specific tool.

**Proposed Revision to Exhibit A Header**:

> The following is an inventory of Contractor's and the Partnership's pre-existing intellectual property as of the Effective Date. **Each item must be identified with sufficient specificity to enable MBM to determine, without ambiguity, what is licensed versus what is owned. Broad category labels (e.g., "tooling," "platform," "pipeline," "libraries") do not constitute sufficient identification; each entry must specify the module name, version or commit reference as of the Effective Date, repository location, and a functional description of the specific capabilities retained by Contractor.**

**Proposed Revised Exhibit A Table Structure**:

| # | Name | Module/Component | Version/Ref | Repository | Functional Description | Owner | License to MBM |

|---|------|-----------------|-------------|------------|----------------------|-------|---------------|

*[Each current broad entry (e.g., "MERIDIAN Daemon Mesh") should be broken into its constituent modules with version references. For example: "mesh/bus.py — NATS message bus abstraction, v1.0, commit abc1234" rather than "MERIDIAN Daemon Mesh — entire system."]*

**Annotation**:

> **Benefit to MBM**: MBM can see exactly what it's licensing versus what it owns. No broad labels creating ambiguous retained-IP footprints. Easier to audit, easier to transition, easier to assess in diligence.

> **Benefit to Contractor**: Module-level specificity actually protects Contractor better than broad labels — it creates a clear, defensible boundary rather than an ambiguous one that could be challenged.

> **Why this matters**: If Exhibit A says "MERIDIAN Daemon Mesh — entire system" and only the NATS bus abstraction layer is actually embedded in MBM's Deliverables, Contractor has unnecessarily claimed the entire platform as retained IP when most of it isn't even used.

MF-5: Exhibit D — Express Acceptance for Major Deliverables, Extended Warranty

**V5 Current**: "If MBM does not provide written notice of acceptance or deficiency within the initial review period, the Deliverable shall be deemed accepted." Warranty is 30 days.

**Problem**: Deemed acceptance on a 10-business-day clock for healthcare software with integrations, security requirements, and PHI-adjacent workflows is too aggressive. 30-day warranty misses latent defects.

**Proposed Replacement for Acceptance Testing section in Exhibit D**:

> **Acceptance Testing**: MBM shall have twenty (20) business days following delivery of each Deliverable to review and either accept or provide written notice of deficiencies. For Deliverables classified as **Major Releases** (initial platform launches, security-sensitive releases, PHI-handling integrations, or Deliverables exceeding 100 hours of billed development), MBM's express written acceptance shall be required — deemed acceptance shall not apply. For all other Deliverables, if MBM does not provide written notice of acceptance or deficiency within the review period, the Deliverable shall be deemed accepted. If MBM provides notice of deficiencies, Contractor shall cure such deficiencies within ten (10) business days at no additional cost, and MBM shall have ten (10) additional business days to re-review. If MBM is actively conducting acceptance testing at the end of the review period and provides written notice that testing is ongoing, the review period shall be automatically extended by ten (10) business days.

> **Warranty Period**: For a period of ninety (90) days following acceptance (whether express or deemed) of each Deliverable, Contractor shall correct at no additional cost to MBM: (a) any material defects or nonconformities with the applicable acceptance criteria; (b) any latent defects — defects that could not reasonably have been discovered during the acceptance testing period through normal testing procedures; (c) any security vulnerabilities in Contractor-written code that are discovered through security assessment or penetration testing; and (d) any failures to meet documented specifications that manifest during normal business use. The warranty period for defects corrected under this paragraph shall restart for the corrected component for a period of thirty (30) days from delivery of the correction.

**Annotation**:

> **Benefit to MBM**: Express acceptance required for major releases — no silent deemed acceptance of security-sensitive healthcare software. Review window doubled to 20 business days. Auto-extension if testing is ongoing. Warranty extended to 90 days with coverage for latent defects, security vulnerabilities, and spec failures. Corrected-defect warranty restart prevents "fix it badly and run out the clock."

> **Benefit to Contractor**: Deemed acceptance still applies for routine deliverables. Review period is defined (not indefinite). Warranty is time-bounded at 90 days. "Material defects" and "documented specifications" provide objective standards.

MF-6: Section 9.5 — Continuous Source Code Access, Not Just at Termination

**V5 Current**: Transition assistance (90 days, 20 hours KT, escrow at MBM's expense) applies only upon termination.

**Problem**: If Contractor controls the only copies of source code, build instructions, and deployment documentation during the term, MBM is dependent even before termination. 20 hours of KT may be inadequate for a complex stack.

**Proposed Addition** (new Section 9.5A — Ongoing Deliverable Access):

> **9.5A Ongoing Deliverable Access.** Throughout the term of this Agreement, Contractor shall provide MBM with continuous access to:

>

> (a) Current source code for all Deliverables and all Contractor Pre-Existing IP embedded in the Deliverables, maintained in a version-controlled repository accessible to MBM's designated personnel;

>

> (b) Build instructions, deployment scripts, environment configuration files, and dependency inventories sufficient for MBM or a third party to independently build, deploy, and operate the Deliverables without Contractor's assistance;

>

> (c) Administrative credentials, API keys, and access tokens for all MBM-owned or MBM-provisioned infrastructure on which Deliverables are deployed;

>

> (d) A current dependency inventory listing all third-party libraries, services, and tools required to operate the Deliverables, including version numbers, license types, and whether each dependency is open-source, proprietary, or Contractor Pre-Existing IP.

>

> Contractor shall update the materials described in this Section 9.5A within ten (10) business days of any material change to the Deliverables or their dependencies. This obligation is independent of, and in addition to, the transition assistance obligations in Section 9.5.

**Proposed Revision to Section 9.5(b)** — expand KT hours:

> (b) Knowledge transfer sessions as reasonably needed (and in any event no fewer than forty (40) hours), during which Contractor shall provide technical briefings to MBM's designated personnel regarding the architecture, operation, maintenance, and independent deployment of the Deliverables, compensated at Contractor's then-current Tier 1 hourly rate;

**Annotation**:

> **Benefit to MBM**: MBM has practical access to everything it needs to operate independently — during the term, not just at termination. If Contractor disappears tomorrow, MBM can continue operating. The dependency inventory prevents hidden lock-in through undisclosed third-party dependencies. KT hours doubled from 20 to 40 minimum, at Tier 1 rate (not Tier 3 advisory).

> **Benefit to Contractor**: Compensated at standard rates for KT. The ongoing access obligation doesn't require Contractor to teach or support — just to make the materials available. Contractor's Pre-Existing IP source code is provided under the Section 3.4 license terms, not as an ownership transfer.

MF-7: Section 5.5 — Resolve Project 1 PHI Status Affirmatively

**V5 Current**: "MBM represents and warrants that Project 1, as scoped in the applicable Statement of Work, does not require Contractor to access, create, receive, maintain, or transmit Protected Health Information." With a "reasonable determination" trigger for mid-stream BAA execution.

**Problem**: If Project 1 includes a patient portal with intake forms and insurance card uploads, calling it "no PHI" is a representation that may already be inaccurate. Waiting for someone to "reasonably determine" PHI is involved mid-stream is reactive.

**Proposed Replacement for Project 1 PHI paragraph**:

> **Project 1 PHI Classification.** The Parties acknowledge that Project 1 (Website Automation) includes functionality that may process individually identifiable health information, including patient intake forms and insurance documentation. The Parties shall determine, as part of the Project 1 Statement of Work (Exhibit D), whether the specific implementation requires Contractor to access, create, receive, maintain, or transmit Protected Health Information as defined under HIPAA. If the SOW determines that Project 1 involves PHI, the BAA (Exhibit B) shall be executed prior to commencement of any PHI-related Services under Project 1, consistent with the hard gate in this Section 5.5. If the SOW determines that Project 1 does not involve PHI (e.g., because patient data is handled entirely within MBM's own systems and Contractor never accesses it), MBM shall include a specific representation to that effect in the SOW, and the automatic BAA trigger in the following paragraph shall apply if that determination later proves inaccurate.

>

> If at any point during the performance of Project 1, either Party determines that the Services involve or will involve PHI (including but not limited to patient intake forms, insurance information, clinical data, or individually identifiable health information), the Parties shall immediately suspend the affected Services and execute the BAA within thirty (30) days before resuming such Services.

**Annotation**:

> **Benefit to MBM**: PHI determination is made affirmatively in the SOW, not left to mid-stream discovery. If Project 1 involves PHI, the BAA is signed before work starts — not after a "reasonable determination" trigger. If it doesn't involve PHI, the representation is made explicitly in the SOW, creating accountability.

> **Benefit to Contractor**: Same protection — if PHI is discovered mid-stream, the suspension-and-cure mechanism still applies. The SOW-level determination gives Contractor clear guidance before starting work.

MF-8: Section 10.7 — Narrow Contractor Exit Right in M&A

**V5 Current**: If MBM assigns to a competitor of Contractor (as mutually determined or by independent attorney), Contractor may terminate on 90 days' notice. Prior IP assignments survive.

**Problem**: Contractor exit right at the moment of an M&A transaction creates leverage against MBM at exactly the wrong time. Even though IP survives, loss of the service relationship and institutional knowledge during a transition is operationally devastating.

**Proposed Replacement for Section 10.7(b)**:

> (b) in the event of an assignment under subsection (a) to an entity that is a direct competitor of Contractor or the Partnership in the multi-agentic PaaS API market (as mutually agreed by the Parties, or if the Parties cannot agree, as determined by the independent attorney process described in Section 2.7(b)), Contractor shall have the right to provide written notice of intent to terminate this Agreement. Upon receipt of such notice, the following transition obligations shall apply before termination becomes effective:

>

> (i) Contractor shall continue to perform Services under all existing Statements of Work for a minimum of one hundred eighty (180) days following the notice (the "Transition Period"), compensated at Contractor's then-current rates;

>

> (ii) During the Transition Period, Contractor shall provide knowledge transfer and transition assistance as described in Section 9.5, with the forty (40)-hour minimum applied regardless of whether the Agreement is also terminating under Section 9;

>

> (iii) All Company IP assignments, licenses, and the ongoing access obligations under Section 9.5A granted prior to and during the Transition Period shall survive in accordance with Section 9.4;

>

> (iv) Contractor shall cooperate in good faith with any replacement contractor or MBM personnel to ensure continuity of the Deliverables during the Transition Period.

**Annotation**:

> **Benefit to MBM**: 180-day transition period (up from 90-day immediate termination) ensures operational continuity during M&A. Full KT and cooperation obligations during transition. MBM has 6 months to find and onboard a replacement — enough for a complex healthcare tech stack.

> **Benefit to Contractor**: Exit right is preserved — Contractor is not forced to work indefinitely for a competitor. Compensated at standard rates during the Transition Period. The 180-day period is bounded, not open-ended.

> **Why this matters**: A 90-day termination right effectively gives the Contractor veto power over MBM's M&A transactions. A 180-day transition with cooperation obligations preserves the exit right while protecting MBM's deal value.

MF-9: Section 3.6 / Exhibit F — Qualify "Shall Govern" to Prevent Infrastructure Overreach

**V5 Current**: "This layered model shall govern the classification of IP components."

**Problem**: "Shall govern" was recommended by Bacherman Martin to give the layered model operative force. But it equally hardens the Infrastructure Layer classification — if Exhibit A/F are broad, MBM has locked in a binding classification that protects overinclusive contractor claims.

**Proposed Qualification** (add to Section 3.6 final paragraph):

> This layered model shall govern the classification of IP components, **subject to the following limitations**: (i) classification of a component in the Infrastructure Layer requires that the component be specifically identified in Exhibit A with the module-level specificity required by that Exhibit; (ii) any component not so identified shall be classified under the Company IP presumption in Section 2.7(f); and (iii) the classification of a component in the Infrastructure Layer does not limit MBM's license rights under Section 3.4(b) with respect to that component. In the event of ambiguity regarding the classification of a specific component, the Parties shall apply this layered model as the primary framework for resolution, with the presumption in Section 2.7(f) applying to any component not specifically identified in Exhibit A, and disputes resolved through the process in Section 2.7.

**Annotation**:

> **Benefit to MBM**: The "shall govern" language now works asymmetrically in MBM's favor: Application Layer classification is binding AND the Infrastructure Layer classification requires module-level specificity in Exhibit A. Broad labels don't get the benefit of binding classification. MBM's expanded license rights (MF-2) are explicitly not limited by the layer classification.

> **Benefit to Contractor**: Properly documented Pre-Existing IP (module-level, versioned, in Exhibit A) still gets full Infrastructure Layer protection. This only hurts the Contractor if Exhibit A is vague — which is fixable by doing the documentation work.

MF-10: Section 8.3 — Expand Liability Cap Carve-Outs

**V5 Current**: Carve-outs for indemnification, confidentiality/HIPAA, IP breach, and willful misconduct/fraud.

**Problem**: The carve-outs may not be comprehensive enough. Security breaches, data restoration costs, equitable relief, and third-party claims should be explicitly excluded from the cap.

**Proposed Replacement for Section 8.3 opening**:

> EXCEPT FOR (A) A PARTY'S INDEMNIFICATION OBLIGATIONS UNDER SECTIONS 8.1 AND 8.2, (B) A PARTY'S BREACH OF ARTICLE 5 (CONFIDENTIALITY) OR SECTION 5.5 (HIPAA COMPLIANCE), (C) CONTRACTOR'S BREACH OF SECTIONS 2.1 THROUGH 2.4 (IP OWNERSHIP AND ASSIGNMENT), (D) A PARTY'S WILLFUL MISCONDUCT OR FRAUD, (E) EITHER PARTY'S OBLIGATION TO PAY COSTS OF DATA RESTORATION, BREACH NOTIFICATION, AND CREDIT MONITORING ARISING FROM A SECURITY INCIDENT ATTRIBUTABLE TO THAT PARTY'S BREACH OF SECTIONS 7.6 OR 7.7, AND (F) EITHER PARTY'S OBLIGATION TO COMPLY WITH EQUITABLE RELIEF (INCLUDING INJUNCTIVE RELIEF AND SPECIFIC PERFORMANCE) ORDERED BY A COURT OF COMPETENT JURISDICTION:

**Annotation**:

> **Benefit to MBM**: Data breach costs (notification, credit monitoring, restoration) are now explicitly uncapped. Equitable relief (injunctions) is explicitly excluded — MBM can get a court order without the cap limiting the scope of relief.

> **Benefit to Contractor**: The new carve-outs are bilateral. If MBM causes a security breach, MBM's costs are equally uncapped.

MF-11: Section 6.5 — Invoice Acceptance Does Not Waive Later Claims

**V5 Current**: "Invoices not disputed in writing within fifteen (15) days of receipt shall be deemed accepted."

**Problem**: Deemed invoice acceptance could be argued to waive MBM's right to challenge hidden overbilling, fraud, or work that later fails acceptance criteria.

**Proposed Addition** (to Section 6.5, after the deemed acceptance sentence):

> Deemed acceptance of an invoice shall constitute acceptance of the invoice for payment purposes only and shall not constitute acceptance of the Deliverables described therein (which is governed by Exhibit D), waiver of any claim for defective or nonconforming work, waiver of any right to audit under Section 3.7, or waiver of any right to seek recovery for fraud, misrepresentation, or material overbilling discovered after the deemed acceptance period.

**Annotation**:

> **Benefit to MBM**: Paying an invoice doesn't mean accepting the work. MBM preserves all remedies for defective work, overbilling, and fraud regardless of whether the invoice was timely disputed.

> **Benefit to Contractor**: Payment predictability is preserved — deemed acceptance still means MBM must pay. The clause only preserves MBM's right to challenge the underlying work, not to withhold payment.

NICE-TO-HAVE REDLINES (6 items)

NH-1: Section 2.5 — Tighten POA Execution Timing for Uncontested Filings

Add to 2.5: "For filings that relate exclusively to Company IP that is not subject to any pending dispute under Section 2.7, Contractor shall execute the requested documents within ten (10) business days."

NH-2: Section 2.6 — Broaden Non-Exercise Covenant Scope

Add to 2.6 after "interfere with MBM's exercise of its rights": "including but not limited to MBM's rights to modify, commercialize, sublicense, create derivative works of, anonymize, and reuse Company IP in any manner consistent with Section 2.3."

NH-3: Section 3.5 — Add Audit Log Retention

Add to 3.5: "Both Parties shall maintain audit logs of repository access, commit history, and development environment activity for a minimum of three (3) years, available for review under the audit rights in Section 3.7."

NH-4: Section 3.7 — Expand Audit Scope

Add to 3.7: "Audit scope shall include, without limitation, verification of Exhibit A completeness, review of dependency maps and build processes, confirmation of what Contractor Pre-Existing IP is actually embedded in the Deliverables, and review of segregation compliance."

NH-5: Section 4.3 — Functional Substitutability Catch

Add to 4.3 after the carve-outs: "Notwithstanding the foregoing carve-outs, if any product, service, or technology developed by Contractor or the Partnership becomes functionally substitutable for the specific Project Deliverables described in an applicable Statement of Work, MBM's right of first refusal shall apply regardless of whether such product was developed as a 'general-purpose tool' under subsection (a)."

NH-6: Section 7.8 — Survival of Claims-Made Coverage

Add to 7.8: "For claims-made policies, each Party shall maintain coverage or an extended reporting period (tail coverage) for a minimum of twenty-four (24) months following termination."
