Summary

MBM IP Agreement v6 establishes ownership rules where MBM owns all deliverables built specifically for them, while the Contractor retains pre-existing tools but grants MBM expanded rights to use, modify, migrate, and sublicense them without further permission.

Decisions Made

  • MBM owns all work products built under signed Statement of Work (SOW)
  • Contractor retains ownership of pre-existing tools but MBM can operate, fix, migrate, test, and share them
  • Exhibit A must list Contractor tools specifically with file names and versions, not vague descriptions
  • Default ownership assumption: if tool not listed in Exhibit A, it belongs to MBM
  • Three-layer ownership model: Infrastructure (Contractor tools) / Application (MBM owns) / Data (MBM owns)
  • Damage liability cap is 2x annual MBM payments or $250,000, except for IP theft, HIPAA violations, or fraud
  • 90-day warranty on deliverables (up from 30 days) including latent defects and security holes
  • HIPAA BAA must be signed before patient data work; Project 1 determines upfront whether patient data is involved

Commitments

  • Contractor to provide 40 hours of training upon contract end at $165/hr (up from 20 hours in v5)
  • Contractor to cooperate with replacement contractor during ownership transfer
  • If MBM acquired by competitor, Contractor must work for minimum 180 days (6 months) with 40 hours training during transition
  • Contractor to maintain source code, build instructions, passwords, and dependency lists accessible to MBM throughout project, updated within 10 business days of changes
  • Both sides to maintain 3-year access logs and conduct annual compliance check-ups
  • Contractor to notify MBM of security incidents within 72 hours

MBM IP Agreement v6 — Simple Breakdown

What Is This Document?

This is a **contract** — a set of rules that two groups agree to follow when working together. Think of it like the rules you agree to before playing a game with someone.

The two groups are:

• **MBM** (Millennial Behavioral Management) — A doctor's office that helps people with their mental health. They need someone to build computer programs for them.

• **The Contractor** (Alexander Mazzei) — A person who builds computer programs. He already has some tools he built before, and he'll use them to build new things for MBM.

**The big question this contract answers:** When the Contractor builds something for MBM, who gets to keep it — and can MBM actually use what they own without needing the Contractor's help forever?

---

The Big Idea Behind v6: "Own the Cake AND the Oven"

Earlier versions of this contract made sure MBM **legally owned** everything built for them. But there was a problem: the Contractor's tools were built into the foundation of the software, and MBM could only "borrow" those tools in a limited way. It's like owning a cake but not the oven it was baked in — you own the cake, but you can't bake a new one, fix the old one, or move it to a different kitchen without asking the baker for permission.

**V6 fixes this.** MBM still owns the cake (the custom software). But now MBM also gets much bigger rights to use the oven — including the right to move to a different kitchen, hire a different baker, let a new business partner use the oven, and test the oven for safety. The Contractor still owns the oven, but MBM can do everything they need to do without calling the Contractor every time.

---

Article 1 — Definitions (What Do These Words Mean?)

Before you play a game, you need to agree on what the words mean. This section is like a dictionary for the contract.

Company IP — "The Stuff MBM Owns"

Anything the Contractor **builds specifically for MBM** belongs to MBM. If MBM asks the Contractor to build a patient sign-in page, that page belongs to MBM — even though the Contractor is the one who built it.

Think of it like hiring someone to paint your house. You bought the paint, you picked the colors, and you're paying them to do the work. When they're done, the painted house is yours — not theirs.

MBM Resources — "MBM's Stuff"

This is a list of things that belong to MBM: their patient information, their medical rules, their computers and cloud accounts that they set up for this project. If the Contractor uses any of MBM's stuff to build something, whatever they build belongs to MBM.

Contractor Pre-Existing IP — "The Contractor's Toolbox"

The Contractor already has his own tools that he built before working with MBM — like a carpenter who brings their own hammer and saw to a job. These tools stay his. MBM gets to **use** them (like borrowing the hammer), but they don't get to **keep** them.

**New in v6**: The Contractor has to describe each tool specifically — not just "my toolbox," but "the Phillips-head screwdriver, model #3, serial number 12345." No vague descriptions allowed. This way, everyone knows exactly what belongs to who.

Hybrid Component — "A Mix of Both"

Sometimes the Contractor uses his own tools AND builds new stuff for MBM on top of them. When that happens:

• His original tool = still his

• The new stuff built on top for MBM = belongs to MBM

It's like if the Contractor brings his own ladder to paint your house. The ladder is still his. But the paint job he did while standing on it? That's yours.

Deliverables — "What MBM Is Paying For"

This is the actual finished work the Contractor creates for MBM — the websites, the apps, the medical tools. Think of it as the final product, like a finished painting you ordered.

General Knowledge — "What You Learn Along the Way"

When the Contractor works for MBM, he'll learn new things — like better ways to write code or how to solve certain problems. He gets to keep that knowledge in his head and use it in the future. But he can't take MBM's specific secrets or the exact things he built for them and use those somewhere else.

It's like a chef who works at a restaurant. They can learn "how to make food taste better" (general knowledge), but they can't take the restaurant's secret recipe and give it to another restaurant (that's the restaurant's stuff).

Article 2 — Who Owns What (The Most Important Part)

Section 2.1 — "MBM Owns Everything They Pay For"

Whatever the Contractor builds for MBM, MBM owns it. Completely. Forever. The Contractor can't use it, copy it, or give it to anyone else.

Section 2.1A — "But Only After a Project Plan Is Signed" (New in v6)

**Before** the Contractor starts any work, both sides have to sign a **Statement of Work (SOW)** — a written plan that says exactly what will be built, how long it will take, and how much it costs. The ownership rule only kicks in for work done under a signed plan.

Think of it like a construction contract: you don't start building until the blueprint is approved and signed. If the Contractor does some side project on his own that has nothing to do with MBM's plan, that doesn't automatically belong to MBM.

**But** — if the Contractor uses MBM's stuff (their patient data, their cloud systems) to build something, it belongs to MBM no matter what, even without a signed plan.

Section 2.4 — "But the Contractor Keeps Their General Skills"

The Contractor doesn't own anything he builds for MBM. But he does get to keep the **general skills** he learned along the way. He just can't take MBM's specific medical workflows, patient data setups, or the exact code he wrote for them.

Section 2.5 — "Signing the Papers"

MBM might need the Contractor to sign papers proving MBM owns things (like patents or copyrights). For simple, uncontested filings, the Contractor has **10 business days** to sign. For more complex situations, he has **20 business days**. If there's a fight about who owns something, MBM has to wait until the fight is settled before making the Contractor sign.

Section 2.6 — "Moral Rights (A Special Rule in Puerto Rico)"

In Puerto Rico, artists and creators have special rights called "moral rights" — the right to be known as the creator, and the right to stop people from changing their work in ways they don't like. These rights can't be given away under Puerto Rico law (Act 55-2012).

So instead, the Contractor agrees: "I won't use these rights to get in MBM's way." MBM can change, sell, sublicense, anonymize, and update the software without asking the Contractor's permission.

Section 2.7 — "What Happens When They Disagree"

If MBM and the Contractor can't agree about who owns something, here's what they do:

1. **Talk it out** — They have 15 business days to try to agree

2. **Bring in an expert** — An independent lawyer looks at it and decides

3. **If the thing is worth less than $50,000** — The expert's decision is final

4. **If the thing is worth more than $50,000** — Either side can appeal, but the loser pays

While they're fighting about it, neither side can sell or give away the disputed thing. But both sides can keep using it so their work doesn't stop.

**New in v6 — The "Default Assumption" Rule**: Unless the Contractor can prove that a specific tool was already on the list (Exhibit A) **before** he used it on the project, everything is assumed to belong to MBM. The Contractor has to prove it's his — MBM doesn't have to prove it's theirs.

Think of it like this: if you find a toy in your house that you don't remember buying, the default assumption is that it's yours until someone shows a receipt proving they bought it and brought it over.

**New in v6 — "Don't Cloud the Title"**: If there's a fight about one specific piece of software, it doesn't affect MBM's ownership of everything else. A disagreement about one puzzle piece doesn't put the whole puzzle in question.

Article 3 — Protecting the Contractor's Own Tools (With MBM's Right to Use Them)

Section 3.4 — "The Contractor's Toolbox Rules" (Major v6 Upgrade)

The Contractor has to tell MBM about every tool he brings to the project (by adding it to a list called "Exhibit A"). He has 30 days to add any new tool. If he forgets to add a tool to the list, it's automatically assumed to belong to MBM until they sort it out.

The big v6 change — what MBM can do with the Contractor's tools:

In v5, MBM could only "operate, maintain, and modify" the Contractor's tools. That's like being allowed to drive a car but not allowed to take it to a different mechanic, get it inspected, or let your family drive it.

In v6, MBM can:

• **Run it** — operate the software as normal

• **Fix it** — maintain and modify

• **Move it** — migrate to a different server, platform, or vendor

• **Test it** — run security checks and safety audits

• **Share it** — let MBM's partners, acquirers, or affiliates use it

• **Hire someone else to work on it** — bring in a different contractor to maintain it

The Contractor still **owns** the tools — but MBM can do everything they need to do without asking permission. It's the difference between renting an apartment where you need the landlord's permission for every little thing, versus a lease that lets you paint the walls, hire your own plumber, and have guests stay over.

Section 3.5 — "Keep Things Separate"

Both sides have to keep MBM's work separate from the Contractor's other work. Separate folders, separate passwords, separate everything. This way, it's always clear what belongs to who.

**New in v6**: Both sides have to keep records of who accessed what for at least 3 years — like a security camera log.

Section 3.6 — "The Three Layers (How the Software Is Organized)"

Think of the software like a three-layer cake:

| Layer | What It Is | Who Owns It |

|-------|-----------|-------------|

| **Bottom Layer (Infrastructure)** | The Contractor's tools and platforms (his "ingredients and oven") | The Contractor owns them; MBM has broad rights to use them |

| **Middle Layer (Application)** | The medical workflows, patient screens, and business rules built specifically for MBM (the "actual cake") | **MBM owns this completely** |

| **Top Layer (Data)** | All patient information and medical records (the "candles and decorations") | **MBM owns this completely** |

This is the **official rule** for deciding who owns what.

**New in v6 — Important Limitations on the Bottom Layer**:

For the Contractor to claim something belongs in the bottom layer (his tools), he has to:

1. **List it specifically** in Exhibit A — with exact file names and version numbers, not vague labels

2. **Have had it before** the project started, or built it independently

If the Contractor didn't list something specifically, it's assumed to belong to MBM (the default assumption from Section 2.7). And even when something IS properly listed in the bottom layer, MBM's expanded use rights (Section 3.4) still apply — the Contractor can't use the layer classification to block MBM from migrating, testing, or getting help from other contractors.

Section 3.7 — "Checking Up on Each Other"

Once a year, either side can check that the other is following the rules about keeping things separate. Like a teacher checking that you actually did your homework instead of just saying you did.

**New in v6**: The check-up can also look at what tools are actually built into MBM's software, what outside libraries are used, and how everything fits together — not just whether files are in the right folders.

---

Article 4 — Playing Fair (No Competing)

Section 4.3 — "Right of First Refusal"

If the Contractor wants to build something that directly competes with what he's building for MBM, he has to tell MBM first and give them a chance to be involved. MBM has 30 business days to decide. If they don't respond, they lose their chance.

**But** — the Contractor can still build general tools for other industries, work for other clients (even in healthcare), and build his own AI platform. The rule only applies to things that are really, really similar to MBM's specific project.

**New in v6 — "No Loopholes"**: If the Contractor builds something he calls a "general tool" but it actually does the same thing as MBM's project, MBM still gets the first-refusal right. You can't get around the rule just by calling a competitor product a "general tool."

---

Article 5 — Keeping Secrets

Section 5.5 — "HIPAA and Patient Privacy"

HIPAA is a law that protects patient health information. Before the Contractor can see or work with any patient data, both sides have to sign a special agreement called a **BAA** (Business Associate Agreement). This is like getting a special security clearance.

**For Project 1** (the website): **New in v6** — Instead of just hoping patient data won't show up, both sides decide upfront in the project plan (SOW) whether Project 1 involves patient data. If it does, the BAA must be signed before that work starts. If it doesn't, MBM puts that in writing in the plan. If patient data shows up later despite the plan saying otherwise, work stops immediately until the BAA is signed.

**For Project 2** (the medical platform): The BAA must be signed before any work starts. No exceptions.

---

Article 6 — Working Together

Section 6.4 — "Bringing in Help"

The Contractor can bring in other people from his team to help, but MBM has to approve each person first. MBM has 10 business days to say yes or no. If they don't respond, it's automatically a yes. Everyone who helps has to sign the same rules about who owns what.

Section 6.5 — "Getting Paid"

The Contractor sends MBM a bill every month. MBM has 30 days to pay. If MBM thinks something on the bill is wrong, they have 15 days to say so — but they still have to pay the parts they agree with. If MBM pays late, they owe extra (1.5% per month, like a late fee on a credit card).

**New in v6 — "Paying the Bill Doesn't Mean You Liked the Work"**: Just because MBM paid for something doesn't mean they accepted that the work was done well. If MBM finds out later that the work was bad or the billing was wrong, they can still challenge it — even after paying. Paying the invoice is about paying for time worked, not approving quality.

---

Article 7 — Promises and Insurance

Section 7.4 — "Where the Contractor Lives and Tax Rules"

The Contractor promises he lives in Puerto Rico. This matters because of special tax rules (Act 60) that both sides benefit from. If the Contractor moves or his tax status changes, he has to tell MBM.

Section 7.6-7.7 — "Keeping Things Safe"

Both sides promise to keep their computers and systems secure. If either side gets hacked or has a security problem, they have to tell the other side within 72 hours.

Section 7.8 — "Insurance"

Both sides have to carry insurance — like car insurance, but for businesses. This protects everyone if something goes really wrong (like a data breach or a lawsuit).

**New in v6**: Insurance coverage has to continue for at least 2 years after the contract ends — because sometimes problems aren't discovered until later.

---

Article 8 — What Happens If Something Goes Wrong

Section 8.3 — "Limits on How Much You Can Owe"

If something goes wrong (like a regular business mistake, not stealing), the most either side can owe the other is **2 times what MBM paid in the last year, or $250,000** — whichever is more.

**But** — there are situations where there's **no limit at all**. These include:

• Stealing MBM's stuff (IP breach)

• Leaking patient data (HIPAA violation)

• Leaking secrets (confidentiality breach)

• Doing something bad on purpose (fraud)

• **New in v6**: Costs from a data breach (like notifying patients, credit monitoring, restoring data)

• **New in v6**: Court orders (if a judge says "stop doing that" or "do this," the dollar limit doesn't apply)

---

Article 9 — Starting, Ending, and In Between

Section 9.1 — "How Long Does This Last?"

The contract runs until a set end date, then automatically renews every year. Either side can end it with 60 days' notice.

Section 9.5 — "Handing Things Over When It Ends"

When the contract ends (for any reason), the Contractor has to:

1. **Give MBM all their stuff** — source code, documents, everything

2. **Teach MBM's team** — at least **40 hours** of training (up from 20 in v5) at the regular coding rate ($165/hr, not the consulting rate), so they can keep things running

3. **Put a copy in a safe** (if MBM asks) — like putting your valuables in a safety deposit box, just in case

4. **New in v6**: **Help the new contractor** — If MBM hires someone to replace the Contractor, the Contractor has to cooperate with them during the handover. No cold shoulders.

The Contractor gets paid for this handover work.

Section 9.5A — "MBM Gets the Keys During the Whole Project, Not Just at the End" (New in v6)

This is one of the most important new rules. **While the project is still going** (not just when it ends), the Contractor has to give MBM access to:

1. **The source code** — the actual instructions that make the software work, kept in a shared folder MBM can see anytime

2. **The instruction manual** — how to build, set up, and run the software without the Contractor's help

3. **The passwords** — all the login information for MBM's own systems

4. **The ingredient list** — a list of every outside tool, library, or service the software depends on

Think of it like buying a car: you don't just get the car at the end — you get the keys, the owner's manual, and the maintenance schedule from day one. If the mechanic disappears, you can still drive the car and take it to a different mechanic.

The Contractor has to update all of this within 10 business days anytime something changes.

Article 10 — General Rules

Section 10.6 — "How to Send Official Messages"

Important messages (like "I'm ending the contract") have to be sent by mail or courier. For less important things (like "let's talk about who owns this code"), email is fine.

Section 10.7 — "What If MBM Gets Bought By Another Company?" (Major v6 Change)

MBM can transfer this contract if they get bought by another company. If that company is a direct competitor of the Contractor's own business, the Contractor can announce he wants to leave.

**But** — he can't just walk out. New in v6:

1. He has to keep working for at least **180 days** (6 months) — enough time for MBM to find and train a replacement

2. During those 6 months, he has to do at least **40 hours** of training with the new team

3. He has to cooperate with the replacement contractor

4. Everything MBM already owns stays with MBM — the Contractor can't take it with him

The old version let the Contractor quit with just 90 days' notice. V6 gives MBM twice as much time and requires the Contractor to actively help with the transition.

Section 10.10 — "What If Something Crazy Happens?"

If something outside anyone's control happens (hurricane, earthquake, pandemic), neither side gets in trouble for not being able to work. But they have to tell each other within 48 hours and try to get back to work as soon as possible. If it lasts more than 90 days, either side can end the contract.

Section 10.11 — "Changing the Plan"

If either side wants to change what they're building, they write up the change, and both sides have to agree in writing. No surprise changes.

The Exhibits (Attachments)

Exhibit A — "The Contractor's Tool List" (Major v6 Change)

A list of 8 tools the Contractor already owns. MBM can use them for this project but doesn't own them.

**New in v6**: Each tool has to be listed **specifically** — not just "my toolbox" but the exact file names, version numbers, and where the code lives. Vague descriptions like "reusable code libraries" aren't good enough anymore. This is like the difference between saying "I brought some tools" versus "I brought a DeWalt DCD771C2 drill, serial number ABC123."

If the Contractor doesn't list a tool specifically enough, it can't be claimed as his.

Exhibit B — "The Patient Privacy Agreement (BAA)"

The special HIPAA agreement. Must be signed before any patient data work. For Project 1, whether patient data is involved is now decided upfront in the project plan.

Exhibit D — "The Project Plan Template" (Major v6 Change)

A form for planning each new part of the project. The v6 version adds several important changes:

**Review time**: MBM now has **20 business days** (up from 10) to test and review each piece of delivered software. If MBM is still testing when the timer runs out, the timer automatically extends by another 10 days.

**Big releases need a signature**: For major releases (like the first version of the platform, anything involving patient data, or anything that took more than 100 hours to build), MBM has to actually sign off and say "yes, this is approved." The software isn't considered accepted just because MBM didn't say "no" in time.

**Longer warranty**: If something breaks, the Contractor fixes it for free for **90 days** (up from 30). The warranty also now covers:

• Problems that were hidden and didn't show up right away (latent defects)

• Security holes in the Contractor's code

• Things that don't match what the plan said they should do

• If the Contractor fixes a bug, the warranty restarts for 30 days on that fix — so you're not stuck with a bad fix just because the warranty clock ran out

Exhibit E — "Tax Rules"

A plan for making sure the money between MBM and the Contractor follows tax rules. Not active yet, but **automatically kicks in** if either side gets audited by the IRS or Puerto Rico's tax office.

Exhibit F — "The Blueprint"

A map showing exactly which parts of the software belong to who, following the three-layer model from Article 3. **New in v6**: This map now lists specific file names and modules (matching Exhibit A), not just broad categories.

The Bottom Line

1. **MBM owns everything built specifically for them** — the websites, apps, medical tools, patient data, and all the custom code — but only for work done under a signed project plan

2. **The Contractor keeps his own tools** — but MBM can use them broadly: run them, fix them, move them, test them, share them with partners, and hire other people to work on them

3. **No vague descriptions** — every tool the Contractor claims as his own must be listed specifically with file names and version numbers

4. **If there's a question about who owns something, the default answer is MBM** — the Contractor has to prove it's his

5. **Patient data is sacred** — whether it's involved is decided upfront in the project plan, not discovered later

6. **MBM gets the keys from day one** — source code, build instructions, passwords, and dependency lists are shared throughout the project, not just at the end

7. **If the Contractor leaves, MBM has 6 months and 40 hours of training** — enough time and help to transition to someone new

8. **If something breaks, the Contractor fixes it free for 90 days** — including hidden problems, security holes, and bad fixes

9. **Paying a bill doesn't mean approving the work** — MBM can always challenge quality, even after payment

10. **Both sides have to be honest and keep good records** — separate work, 3-year audit logs, annual check-ups, and insurance for everyone
