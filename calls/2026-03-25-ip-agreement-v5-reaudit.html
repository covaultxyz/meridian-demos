PHASE 4: REMAINING RISK

Ranked by likelihood x impact:

Risk 1: Rates Never Get Filled In (Likelihood: MEDIUM, Impact: HIGH)

**What could go wrong**: The agreement is signed with *[RATE]* placeholders, or rate negotiation stalls indefinitely, delaying Project 2 and creating ambiguity about Project 1 going-forward compensation.

**Trigger**: Omar and Alex disagree on rates, particularly the Project 2 premium for HIPAA/clinical complexity.

**Consequence**: Agreement is unenforceable on compensation. Creates leverage asymmetry: work continues at undefined rates, disputes arise retroactively.

**Mitigation**: Contractor can control this — don't sign until numbers are in. The rate-setting methodology in Exhibit C provides a framework for the negotiation (arm's-length, market-comparable, documented rationale).

Risk 2: Exhibit I Becomes a Documentation Black Hole (Likelihood: MEDIUM, Impact: MEDIUM)

**What could go wrong**: The Technical Architecture Document is an ambitious deliverable (system diagram, component inventory with IP classification, boundary annotations, dependency map). If Contractor creates it dutifully but it's never reviewed by MBM or updated after the first version, it becomes an outdated artifact that misleads rather than clarifies IP classification disputes.

**Trigger**: Quarterly review meetings don't happen. Components evolve but Exhibit I doesn't. A dispute arises and the parties rely on a stale document.

**Consequence**: §2.8 references Exhibit I as the evidentiary baseline. A stale Exhibit I could miscategorize components and create disputes rather than prevent them.

**Mitigation**: The update schedule in Exhibit I (I.3) ties updates to quarterly reviews, new component introductions, and escrow deposits. But enforcement depends on the Exhibit D.3 quarterly review meetings actually happening. **Recommendation**: Add a clause that Exhibit I automatically reflects the version deposited in the most recent escrow, creating a self-enforcing update mechanism.

Risk 3: Ayala PC Refuses to Sign Exhibit G (Likelihood: LOW-MEDIUM, Impact: HIGH)

**What could go wrong**: Ayala PC's attorney advises against signing an acknowledgment letter that disclaims IP ownership, particularly if Ayala PC funded the development.

**Trigger**: Ayala PC views the payment as purchasing services/IP for itself, not as advancing funds on MBM's behalf.

**Consequence**: The Ayala PC payment routing remains undocumented. In an IRS audit, this is the weakest link in the Act 60 chain. Without Exhibit G, MBM's claim that it was "at all times the intended contracting party" rests on the MSA alone.

**Mitigation**: This is external to Contractor — MBM must obtain it. The "promptly after execution" language is the concern; it should ideally be "before execution" only. However, forcing MBM to produce it pre-signing could delay the entire agreement. **This is a risk the Contractor must accept with eyes open** or insist on pre-signing procurement.

Risk 4: Insurance Costs Are Prohibitive for Solo Contractor (Likelihood: MEDIUM, Impact: MEDIUM)

**What could go wrong**: $1M Professional Liability + $1M Cyber Liability insurance for a solo contractor may cost $3,000-8,000/year. If the engagement value doesn't justify this cost, Contractor may resist or carry inadequate coverage.

**Trigger**: Project 2 commencement (insurance is a prerequisite per Exhibit F-2).

**Consequence**: Project 2 delayed, or Contractor carries coverage that's technically compliant but practically insufficient.

**Mitigation**: §7.8 already includes a good-faith cost allocation discussion. **Recommendation**: Consider whether MBM should reimburse Contractor's incremental insurance cost as a Project 2 expense, since the coverage is required by MBM's engagement. This is common in HIPAA contractor relationships.

Risk 5: Retroactive Assignment Challenged on Consideration Grounds (Likelihood: LOW, Impact: HIGH)

**What could go wrong**: A court determines that the retroactive assignment in §2.2 is unenforceable because: (a) consideration for Pre-Effective-Date work flowed from Ayala PC, not MBM; (b) no IP assignment agreement existed during the April 2025 – Effective Date period; (c) Contractor's consent to retroactive assignment was given under economic duress (need for ongoing engagement).

**Trigger**: An IP dispute where MBM claims ownership of Prior Work and Contractor challenges the retroactive assignment.

**Consequence**: Prior Work (Project 1 website) may not be legally assigned to MBM. MBM would need to rely on implied assignment or constructive trust theories.

**Mitigation**: Exhibit G (Ayala Acknowledgment) strengthens the consideration chain. Exhibit H catalogues Prior Work with specificity. §2.2(a) asserts mutual understanding. §2.2(c) asserts prior compensation. Combined, these create a strong (though not airtight) foundation. The risk is LOW because both parties currently agree MBM should own this IP — the challenge would only arise in a hostile post-termination scenario.
