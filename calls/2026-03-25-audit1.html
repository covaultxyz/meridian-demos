⏺ AUDIT: MBM IP Ownership Agreement v3

Parties: MBM (Millennial Behavioral Management, LLC) vs. Alexander Mazzei (Contractor)

OVERALL ASSESSMENT: Solid framework, but several provisions need tightening before signing

The agreement does a decent job establishing the IP boundary between MBM work and the Partnership (Mazzei/Bitler). The attorney clearly understood the dual-hat situation. However,

there are specific provisions that tilt heavily toward MBM and create ambiguity that could bite you.

---

RED FLAGS (address before signing)

1. Section 2.5 — Irrevocable Power of Attorney (p.5)

▎ "Contractor hereby irrevocably appoints MBM as Contractor's attorney-in-fact to execute such documents on Contractor's behalf if Contractor fails or refuses to do so within ten

(10) business days"

Problem: This gives MBM the power to sign IP assignment documents in your name if you don't respond within 10 business days. The word "irrevocable" is the issue — even after

termination, MBM retains this power. If there's ever a dispute about whether something is Company IP vs. Pre-Existing IP, MBM can unilaterally sign away the disputed IP on your

behalf.

Recommendation: Make this power revocable upon termination. Add: "This power of attorney shall terminate upon expiration or termination of this Agreement." Also extend the response

window to 20-30 business days.

2. Section 4.3 — Right of First Refusal (p.8)

▎ "If Contractor or the Partnership develops or contemplates developing any product, service, or technology in the medical or healthcare administration software domain..."

Problem: "Healthcare administration software domain" is extremely broad. MERIDIAN/AgentVox's multi-agentic PaaS could serve any industry, but the moment it touches healthcare admin —

even as one of many verticals — you'd owe MBM a right of first refusal. This effectively gives MBM a veto on the Partnership entering the healthcare market independently.

Recommendation: Narrow the scope. Replace "medical or healthcare administration software domain" with something like "products that directly compete with the specific functionality

of the Project Deliverables (as defined in the applicable Statement of Work)". The current language captures too much of the addressable market.

3. Section 1.1 — Company IP Definition (p.3)

▎ Clause (c): "created using MBM's resources, data, or specifications"

Problem: "Resources" is undefined and dangerously broad. If MBM provides a laptop, cloud credits, or even office space, anything you create on those resources — even personal

projects during off hours — could be claimed as Company IP under this clause.

Recommendation: Define "resources" narrowly or add: "For purposes of clause (c), 'resources' means MBM's proprietary software, proprietary data sets, and documented technical

specifications, and does not include general-purpose computing equipment, cloud services, or development tools."

4. Exhibit A — Pre-Existing IP Inventory (p.17)

Problem: Only 2 items listed, one is a placeholder. This is dangerously thin. The agreement says (Sec 7.1(e)) that you represent you've disclosed all Pre-Existing IP. Anything not in

Exhibit A is vulnerable to being claimed as Company IP if MBM argues it was "created in connection with the Services."

MERIDIAN itself is not listed. The MIG, NATS mesh, Neo4j memory graph, daemon architecture, pulse system, all the orchestrator/brain/mesh code — none of this is inventoried. If any

of these tools touch MBM work (and they will, per Section 3.4), the customization layer becomes Company IP while the base tools remain yours — but only if they're in Exhibit A.

Recommendation: Before signing, add a comprehensive Exhibit A including at minimum:

• MERIDIAN distributed daemon system (architecture, orchestrator, brain, mesh)

• MIG (Memory Intelligence Graph) — Neo4j knowledge system

• AgentVox / multi-agentic PaaS platform

• All general-purpose frameworks, libraries, deployment scripts

• Voice/meeting intelligence pipeline

• Any reusable components you've built

---

YELLOW FLAGS (negotiate if possible)

5. Section 2.4 — No Retained Rights (p.5)

▎ "Contractor shall retain no rights, licenses, claims, or interests of any kind in or to any Company IP"

Issue: You can't even reference your own work in a portfolio or use knowledge gained to improve your general skills applied to Company IP specifically. This is standard but

aggressive — most contractor agreements allow a portfolio reference right.

Suggestion: Add a limited portfolio/reference exception: "Notwithstanding the foregoing, Contractor may reference the existence (but not the substance) of the Project in marketing

materials and professional portfolio."

6. Section 2.6 — Moral Rights Waiver (p.6)

Issue: Complete waiver of attribution and integrity rights. Puerto Rico follows US copyright law where moral rights are limited anyway, but this is a total surrender. Reasonable in a

work-for-hire context, but worth noting.

7. Section 3.4 — Independent Contractor Tools (p.6)

▎ "MBM is granted a perpetual, irrevocable, royalty-free, non-exclusive license to use any such tools solely to the extent embedded in or necessary for the operation, maintenance,

and modification of the Deliverables"

Issue: This is a perpetual, irrevocable license to your tools that survive termination. The "necessary for operation, maintenance, and modification" scope is reasonable, but

"modification" could mean MBM builds new features on top of your embedded tools without paying you.

Suggestion: Add "but not for the development of new, standalone products or services" after "modification of the Deliverables."

8. Section 9.1 — Auto-Renewal (p.13)

▎ "automatically renew for successive periods of twelve (12) months unless either Party provides written notice of non-renewal at least thirty (30) days prior"

Issue: Standard, but the 30-day window is short for the Contractor to notice. If you miss the window, you're locked in for another 12 months. Consider extending to 60 days.

9. Section 10.7 — Asymmetric Assignment (p.14)

▎ "Contractor may not assign... MBM may freely assign in connection with a merger, acquisition..."

Issue: If MBM gets acquired, the acquirer inherits all rights over your IP assignments and the perpetual tool license — and you have no say. This is standard but worth understanding.

---

GREEN FLAGS (well-drafted provisions in your favor)

10. Section 3.2 — General Knowledge Carve-Out (p.6)

Strong provision. You and the Partnership can "freely develop and commercialize their own multi-agentic PaaS platform, tools, and services" as long as they don't incorporate Company

IP. This is the key protection for MERIDIAN/AgentVox.

11. Section 3.3 — No Non-Compete (p.6)

Explicit statement that you can work in healthcare with other clients. Critical for future business development.

12. Section 7.3 — MBM Act 60 Representations (p.11)

MBM represents their Act 60 decree is current and in good standing. This protects you if their tax status is later challenged — they bore the representation burden.

13. Section 7.5 — MBM Ongoing Compliance (p.11)

MBM must maintain segregated accounting and cooperate on transfer pricing. Good for your Act 60 compliance.

14. Section 8.2 — MBM Indemnifies You (p.12)

MBM indemnifies you for IP infringement claims arising from their use/commercialization of Company IP. Reciprocal protection.

15. Exhibit D — Best Practice Recommendations (p.21-23)

Well-thought-out practical guidance for both sides. The IRS scrutiny section on Act 60 enforcement is especially relevant.

---

CRITICAL ITEMS FOR DR. OMAR DISCUSSION

1. Exhibit A must be comprehensive before signing. This is the single most important action item. Vague entries = vague boundaries = disputes later.

2. Right of First Refusal scope (4.3) is a strategic constraint on the Partnership. Omar should weigh in on whether "healthcare administration software domain" conflicts with any

planned AgentVox verticals.

3. Power of Attorney (2.5) should be revocable and time-limited. This is non-standard in its current form.

4. Exhibit B (BAA) is empty. If Project 2 (Clinical Platform) involves any PHI, the BAA needs to exist before work begins. HIPAA violations are strict liability. This is a compliance

gap.

5. Exhibit C (Compensation) is blank. Don't sign until rates are locked in. The agreement references hourly compensation but the schedule is empty.

6. Exhibit E (Transfer Pricing) is a dormant landmine — MBM can activate it unilaterally at any time. Understand the implications for your invoicing structure before agreeing.

---

BOTTOM LINE

The structure is sound — the dual-track (MBM IP vs. Partnership/Pre-Existing IP) is well-conceived and the attorney clearly understood the Act 60 dimensions. But the execution

details need work:
