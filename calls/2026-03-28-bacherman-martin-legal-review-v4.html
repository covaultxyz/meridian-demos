1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This counter-proposal is a materially sophisticated revision of the original v3 agreement. It preserves MBM's absolute IP ownership architecture while introducing bilateral protections, operational clarity, and regulatory compliance mechanisms that the original agreement lacked.

**Key Assessment**: The v4 revisions are, on the whole, improvements to the enforceability and institutional quality of the agreement. Several provisions in the original v3 -- notably the blanket moral rights waiver (Section 2.6), the undefined "resources" trigger in the Company IP definition (Section 1.1(c)), and the absence of a BAA execution gate (Section 5.5) -- represent genuine legal vulnerabilities that the counter-proposal corrects. However, the counter-proposal also introduces three material shifts in negotiating position that MBM's counsel must evaluate: (1) the conversion of Exhibit E from unilateral to mutual activation; (2) the competitor-assignment termination right in Section 10.7; and (3) the ROFR scope narrowing in Section 4.3.

The document is presented as a redline/annotation rather than a clean restated agreement. This is appropriate for negotiation but will require consolidation into a single, clean instrument before execution.

**Overall Professional Quality**: High. The drafting demonstrates awareness of Puerto Rico-specific legal requirements (Act 55-2012 moral rights, Act 60 compliance, PR Civil Code), federal regulatory frameworks (HIPAA, 42 CFR Part 2), and commercial best practices. The annotations are honest about which provisions primarily benefit the Contractor. This transparency is unusual and strategically sound -- it reduces the probability of adversarial pushback.

3.2 Provisions Where Disclosure Requires Scrutiny

FINDING 1 -- Section 2.6 (Moral Rights Waiver)

The annotation claims the revision is "actually stronger than the original blanket waiver." This is a defensible legal argument but could be misleading to a non-attorney reader. The revision is not "stronger" in the sense of granting MBM more rights -- it is "stronger" in the sense of being more likely to survive judicial scrutiny. A blanket waiver, if enforceable, would be broader. The revision is more *durable*, not more *expansive*. MBM's counsel should understand this distinction.

**Assessment**: Not a mismatch, but the annotation could be read as advocacy rather than neutral description. Acceptable in a counter-proposal context.

FINDING 2 -- Section 4.3 (ROFR)

The annotation states the narrower ROFR is "more enforceable in court." This is accurate under PR law (overly broad ROFRs are disfavored as restraints on trade) but understates the magnitude of the concession. The original ROFR covered "any product, service, or technology in the medical or healthcare administration software domain." The revision limits it to "substantially similar to and directly competitive with the specific Project Deliverables." The three carve-outs (general-purpose tools, other client work, Partnership PaaS) effectively remove the Partnership's AI platform from ROFR scope entirely.

**Assessment**: The annotation is legally accurate but strategically understated regarding the scope reduction. MBM's attorney should evaluate whether the narrower scope is acceptable given MBM's competitive concerns.

FINDING 3 -- Exhibit E (Transfer Pricing)

The annotation honestly labels this a "Trade-off" and states MBM "loses the unilateral right to activate transfer pricing." This is the most significant concession in the entire counter-proposal. Under the original, MBM could unilaterally impose a transfer pricing methodology at any time. Under the revision, activation requires mutual written agreement.

**Assessment**: The annotation is honest. But the practical implication is significant: if the parties cannot agree on transfer pricing methodology, MBM cannot compel compliance. This could create IRS/Hacienda exposure if the parties' transactions are later characterized as lacking arm's-length pricing.

3.3 Disclosure Match Grade

**STRONG** -- No material mismatches between operative language and annotations. Two instances where annotations could be read as strategic framing rather than neutral description, but this is expected in counter-proposal context. One material concession (Exhibit E) is honestly disclosed.

4.3 Provisions Vulnerable to Hostile Counterparty Attorney

**VULNERABILITY 1**: Section 3.6 -- "shall serve as a guide." A hostile attorney for either side could argue this language is precatory, not operative, and that the layered model has no binding force. This would revert all classification disputes to the bare definitions in Articles 1 and 3, eliminating the practical benefit of the architecture reference.

**VULNERABILITY 2**: Section 2.7(c) -- "$50,000 estimated commercial value" threshold. "Estimated commercial value" is undefined. Who estimates? By what methodology? A hostile attorney could argue every dispute exceeds $50K to avoid the binding recommendation.

**VULNERABILITY 3**: Exhibit A Item #2 -- "MERIDIAN Daemon Mesh" described as including "orchestrator, brain, mesh, and collaboration layers; NATS messaging infrastructure; pulse sentinel system; blue/green deployment system." This description is extremely broad. If any component of the MERIDIAN system is used in MBM's project, the entire system could arguably be "embedded in" the Deliverables, triggering the perpetual license under Section 3.4. MBM's attorney should evaluate whether this license scope is acceptable.

**VULNERABILITY 4**: Section 10.7(b) -- "direct competitor of Contractor or the Partnership in the multi-agentic PaaS API market (as reasonably determined by Contractor)." The phrase "as reasonably determined by Contractor" gives the Contractor sole discretion to define the competitive market. This could be exercised opportunistically.

**VULNERABILITY 5**: Section 8.3(ii) -- 2x aggregate liability cap tied to "twelve (12) month period immediately preceding the event giving rise to the claim." If the relationship is new, the fee base may be small, resulting in an effective cap that is inadequate. For a 6-month engagement at $165/hr and 40 hours/month, the cap would be approximately $79,200 -- potentially insufficient for significant IP or data breach claims that fall outside the carve-outs.

4.4 Puerto Rico Judicial Scrutiny

Puerto Rico operates under a mixed common law/civil law system. The PR Civil Code (Codigo Civil de Puerto Rico, 2020 revision) governs contract interpretation. Key considerations:

1. **Contract interpretation**: PR follows the intent-of-the-parties doctrine (Art. 1233 CC PR). The detailed annotations in the counter-proposal, while not part of the operative agreement, could be used as extrinsic evidence of intent if incorporated by reference or preserved as a negotiation record.

2. **Adhesion contracts**: PR courts scrutinize contracts of adhesion more aggressively than mainland courts. This agreement is negotiated between sophisticated parties, so adhesion concerns are minimal, but the asymmetry of certain provisions (e.g., irrevocable POA, moral rights waiver) could draw scrutiny if the Contractor lacks independent counsel.

3. **Good faith obligation**: PR Civil Code imposes a duty of good faith in all contracts (Art. 1210 CC PR). The "good faith" provisions throughout the agreement (Sections 4.2, 5.5, 7.5) are consistent with this statutory requirement.

4. **Moral rights**: As correctly noted in the counter-proposal, PR Act 55-2012 makes moral rights non-waivable. The non-exercise covenant approach is the best available mitigation, but PR courts have not yet tested this specific mechanism. Risk is LOW but nonzero.

7. ACT 60 COMPLIANCE ANALYSIS

7.1 Structural Assessment

Both parties operate under Act 60-2019 (Puerto Rico Incentives Code) export services decrees. The agreement structure is generally sound for Act 60 purposes, with the following observations:

**COMPLIANT ELEMENTS**:

1. **Contractor residency representation** (Section 7.4): Explicitly requires bona fide PR residency and notification of residency changes. This is essential for income-sourcing characterization under Act 60.

2. **MBM decree representations** (Section 7.3): Comprehensive -- covers decree validity, DDEC/OIN good standing, filing compliance, and absence of pending investigations. Correctly notes the pending SaaS/IT amendment.

3. **Services performed from PR** (Section 7.4(b)): Establishes PR-sourcing of income.

4. **Segregated accounting** (Section 7.5(d)): Required for Act 60 compliance audits.

5. **Transfer pricing framework** (Exhibit E): References IRC Section 482 and PR Internal Revenue Code. The separate-document approach is advisable.

7.2 Risk Areas

RISK 1 -- Exhibit E Mutual Activation

Under the original v3, MBM could unilaterally activate the transfer pricing framework. The v4 revision requires mutual agreement. If the IRS or Hacienda challenges the arm's-length nature of the contractor fees, the absence of a formal transfer pricing study could be problematic. The rates in Exhibit C ($165/$200/$275) are market-benchmarked, which provides some defense, but a formal transfer pricing study would provide stronger protection.

**Recommended mitigation**: Either (a) revert to unilateral activation by either party (not just MBM), or (b) add a clause requiring mandatory activation if either party receives notice of an IRS or Hacienda audit, investigation, or inquiry relating to transfer pricing or income sourcing.

RISK 2 -- Contractor as Individual vs. Entity

The Contractor is identified as "Alexander Mazzei, an individual residing in Puerto Rico." For Act 60 purposes, the individual must hold their own Act 60 decree. The agreement does not reference the Contractor's Act 60 decree status. If the Contractor operates through a PR entity (LLC, S-corp) that holds the Act 60 decree, the agreement should name that entity. If the Contractor holds an individual decree, the agreement should reference it.

**Recommended fix**: Add a Contractor representation mirroring Section 7.3, or at minimum, a representation that the Contractor holds a valid Act 60 decree (if applicable) and that the engagement structure is consistent with the Contractor's decree terms.

RISK 3 -- Partnership IP and Act 60 Sourcing

The Partnership (Mazzei/Bitler) is described as developing a "Multi-Agentic PaaS API Platform." If Bitler is not a PR resident and the Partnership operates across jurisdictions, the Pre-Existing IP in Exhibit A may have been developed partially outside PR. This creates sourcing questions if any of that IP is later licensed to MBM under Section 3.4. The license is described as "perpetual, irrevocable, royalty-free" -- i.e., no ongoing royalty payments -- so transfer pricing is less of a concern for the license itself. However, if MBM later becomes a commercial customer of the Partnership's PaaS API (as contemplated in Exhibit A Item #1), those payments would require arm's-length pricing analysis.

7.3 Act 60 Compliance Grade

**MODERATE-STRONG** -- The core structure is sound, but the Exhibit E mutual activation and the missing Contractor Act 60 decree representation create moderate risk. The rates being market-benchmarked provides baseline defense even without a formal transfer pricing study.

8.1 BAA Gate Assessment

The Section 5.5 hard gate is **excellent** and exceeds what most IP/contractor agreements provide. Key features:

1. **Absolute prohibition**: "No Services related to Project 2...or any engagement involving access to, creation of, or processing of PHI shall commence until Exhibit B (BAA) has been fully executed."

2. **Independence from main agreement**: "This gate applies regardless of whether this Agreement has been signed and is in effect."

3. **Good faith timeline**: 60-day negotiation period prevents indefinite delay.

4. **BAA supremacy clause**: In case of conflict, BAA controls for PHI matters.

**Assessment**: This is a properly constructed HIPAA compliance gate. It would satisfy OCR enforcement review.

8.3 Psychiatric-Specific Gaps

**GAP 1**: The BAA summary mentions "SUD counseling notes" as a category from the 2024 Final Rule, but does not specify whether these are treated differently from general SUD records. Under the Final Rule, SUD counseling notes receive enhanced protections similar to psychotherapy notes. The BAA should explicitly map the handling requirements for each category.

**GAP 2**: The BAA summary does not mention whether the platform will handle court-ordered disclosures of SUD records (42 CFR Section 2.61-2.67). Given that MBM is a psychiatric practice, court-ordered disclosures for involuntary commitment or guardianship proceedings are foreseeable. The BAA should address the technical mechanism for responding to such orders.

**GAP 3**: The BAA summary does not address the intersection of PR Act 81-2019 (Ley de Proteccion de Datos Personales de Puerto Rico) with HIPAA. Act 81-2019 creates additional data protection obligations that may exceed HIPAA in certain respects. The BAA Recitals mention Act 81-2019 but the summary does not detail how conflicts between the two frameworks are resolved.

8.4 HIPAA/42 CFR Part 2 Grade

**STRONG** -- The BAA gate is properly constructed. The BAA framework as summarized is comprehensive and current (incorporating the Feb 2024 Final Rule, Jan 2025 NPRM, and NJ-specific requirements). Three psychiatric-specific gaps should be addressed in the full BAA text but do not undermine the framework's overall adequacy. This is materially better than the original v3, which had a placeholder BAA with no substantive content.

**Rate Defensibility**: The rates are documented with specific source citations (Robert Half 2026, Dice 2025, Glassdoor, ZipRecruiter, ContractRates.fyi). The 1099 multiplier (1.4-1.5x W2 equivalent) is standard. The healthcare/HIPAA premium (20-30%) and AI/ML premium (17.7%) are within published ranges.

**Act 60 Annotation**: The counter-proposal correctly notes that "Act 60 tax advantages benefit Contractor's take-home; they do not reduce the market rate for Services." This is a sound position -- transfer pricing analysis under IRC Section 482 uses the arm's-length standard, which is based on market rates, not after-tax outcomes.

**Assessment**: The rates are **defensible**. They are at or below market for the stated skill set. MBM is not overpaying relative to market. The multi-tier structure provides transparency and reduces billing disputes.

9.2 Liability Cap Assessment

The 2x annual fees cap (Section 8.3(ii)) is within the standard range for contractor agreements (1x-3x is typical). The carve-outs for IP breach, confidentiality breach, HIPAA violations, and willful misconduct are essential and correctly specified.

**Concern**: As noted in Section 4.3 (Risk Surface), the 2x cap may be low for a new engagement with limited fee history. If MBM bills $40,000 in the first six months and a covered (non-carve-out) breach occurs, the cap is approximately $80,000.

**Recommendation**: Consider adding a floor to the liability cap: "in no event less than $250,000."

9.3 Insurance Minimums Assessment

| Coverage | Minimum | Market Standard | Assessment |

|---|---|---|---|

| CGL | $1M/occurrence | $1-2M/occurrence | ADEQUATE |

| Tech E&O | $1M/occurrence | $1-2M/occurrence | ADEQUATE |

| Cyber liability | $2M/occurrence (PHI-triggered) | $2-5M for healthcare | ADEQUATE for engagement size |

**Concern**: No aggregate limit is specified. Per-occurrence limits without aggregate limits could result in exhaustion across multiple small claims. Standard practice is to specify both per-occurrence and annual aggregate.

**Concern**: No requirement for MBM to be named as additional insured on the CGL policy. Standard for enterprise engagements.

9.4 Payment Terms Assessment

Net 30 is standard. The 1.5% monthly late-payment interest (18% annualized) is within market range and below Puerto Rico's usury ceiling. The 15-day deemed acceptance for undisputed invoices is fair and bilateral.

**Missing**: No provision for disputed invoice resolution. If MBM disputes an invoice within 15 days, what happens? The agreement should specify a process: good-faith discussion, escalation, and payment of undisputed portions pending resolution.

9.5 Commercial Terms Grade

**MODERATE-STRONG** -- Rates are defensible. Liability cap structure is sound but may need a floor. Insurance minimums are adequate but missing aggregate limits and additional-insured requirements. Payment terms are fair but missing a dispute resolution mechanism for contested invoices.

10. RECOMMENDATIONS (TOP 10)

Ranked by priority. Each cites the specific section, the issue, and the fix.

Priority 1 (CRITICAL -- Address Before Execution)

REC-1: Exhibit E -- Add Mandatory Activation Trigger for Tax Authority Inquiries

• **Section**: Exhibit E (Transfer Pricing Study Framework)

• **Issue**: Mutual activation requirement creates deadlock risk if either party refuses to engage. If the IRS or Hacienda challenges transfer pricing, the absence of a formal study could expose both parties.

• **Fix**: Add: "Notwithstanding the foregoing, activation of Exhibit E shall be mandatory upon either Party's receipt of (a) a notice of audit, investigation, or inquiry from the IRS, Hacienda, DDEC, or OIN relating to transfer pricing, income sourcing, or Act 60 compliance; or (b) a written request from a qualified tax advisor that a transfer pricing study is necessary to support the Party's tax filing position. In such event, the receiving Party shall notify the other Party within five (5) business days, and the Parties shall cooperate to activate Exhibit E within thirty (30) days."

REC-2: BAA Execution -- Do Not Sign Main Agreement Without Simultaneous BAA

• **Section**: 5.5, Exhibit B

• **Issue**: The BAA gate is correctly designed, but there is a practical risk: the main agreement is signed, Project 1 (Website Automation) begins, and the BAA is never executed. Project 1 involves a patient portal with "login, intake forms, insurance card upload" (Exhibit F P1-1), which may involve PHI depending on implementation. If Project 1 involves any patient data, the BAA gate is triggered.

• **Fix**: (a) Execute the BAA simultaneously with the main agreement, or (b) add a representation that Project 1 does not involve access to, creation of, or processing of PHI, with an automatic BAA trigger if that representation becomes inaccurate.

REC-3: Section 3.6 -- Strengthen Layered Architecture Language

• **Section**: 3.6 (Layered Architecture Acknowledgment)

• **Issue**: "Shall serve as a guide" language is precatory and could be argued to have no operative force.

• **Fix**: Replace "This layered model shall serve as a guide for the classification of IP components in the event of ambiguity" with "This layered model shall govern the classification of IP components. In the event of ambiguity regarding the classification of a specific component, the Parties shall apply this layered model as the primary framework for resolution."

Priority 2 (HIGH -- Should Address Before Execution)

REC-4: Section 2.7(c) -- Define "Estimated Commercial Value"

• **Section**: 2.7(c) (IP Classification Disputes)

• **Issue**: "Estimated commercial value" is undefined. No methodology specified. Creates an incentive for the escalating party to inflate the value to avoid binding recommendation.

• **Fix**: Add: "For purposes of this Section 2.7(c), 'estimated commercial value' shall be determined by the independent attorney as part of the classification recommendation, using the fair market value of the disputed IP as of the date of the dispute notice, based on (i) the cost of development, (ii) comparable licensing or assignment transactions, or (iii) discounted future revenue attributable to the disputed IP, whichever methodology the independent attorney determines to be most appropriate."

REC-5: Section 10.7(b) -- Replace Subjective Competitor Test

• **Section**: 10.7(b) (Assignment -- competitor termination right)

• **Issue**: "Direct competitor of Contractor or the Partnership in the multi-agentic PaaS API market (as reasonably determined by Contractor)" gives Contractor sole discretion to define the competitive market.

• **Fix**: Replace "as reasonably determined by Contractor" with "as mutually agreed by the Parties, or if the Parties cannot agree, as determined by the independent attorney process described in Section 2.7(b)."

REC-6: Add Force Majeure Clause

• **Section**: New Section 10.10 (or equivalent)

• **Issue**: No force majeure provision. PR is subject to hurricanes, earthquakes, and grid failures. COVID-era case law has made force majeure clauses more important, not less.

• **Fix**: Add standard force majeure clause covering natural disasters, pandemics, government actions, utility failures, cyberattacks, and acts of terrorism. Include notification requirement (within 48 hours), mitigation obligation, and termination right if force majeure continues beyond 90 days.

Priority 3 (MODERATE -- Recommended Before Execution)

REC-7: Add Acceptance Testing Procedure

• **Section**: New section or Exhibit D (Statement of Work template)

• **Issue**: No mechanism for formal acceptance or rejection of Deliverables. Without acceptance criteria, "completion" is undefined.

• **Fix**: Either (a) add an acceptance testing provision to Article 6 specifying a review period (e.g., 10 business days), acceptance criteria (e.g., conformance to specifications, absence of material defects), and deemed-acceptance mechanism; or (b) include an SOW template as Exhibit D with per-deliverable acceptance criteria.

REC-8: Section 8.3(ii) -- Add Liability Cap Floor

• **Section**: 8.3(ii) (Aggregate Liability Cap)

• **Issue**: 2x trailing 12-month fees may be inadequate for a new engagement with limited fee history.

• **Fix**: Add: "but in no event less than Two Hundred Fifty Thousand Dollars ($250,000)."

REC-9: Section 7.8 -- Strengthen Insurance Verification

• **Section**: 7.8 (Insurance)

• **Issue**: "Certificates of insurance upon request" is passive. No required delivery frequency, no named-insured or additional-insured requirement, no aggregate limits specified.

• **Fix**: (a) Require annual certificate delivery without request; (b) require MBM to be named as additional insured on Contractor's CGL policy; (c) specify both per-occurrence and annual aggregate limits; (d) require 30-day advance notice of policy cancellation or material change.

REC-10: Add Contractor Act 60 Decree Representation

• **Section**: New Section 7.4(d) or addition to existing 7.4

• **Issue**: Agreement does not reference whether Contractor holds an Act 60 decree. If Contractor's Act 60 status affects income sourcing characterization of payments, MBM's decree compliance could be implicated.

• **Fix**: Add representation: "Contractor represents that [Contractor holds / does not hold] an individual export services decree under Act 60-2019, decree number [____]. Contractor shall promptly notify MBM of any change in decree status, and the Parties shall cooperate in good faith to ensure the engagement structure remains consistent with both Parties' respective Act 60 compliance obligations."
