Perspective 4: Reusability (Platform)

The terminal isn't just for Rankona. Carl's JV channel targets $100M+/year sellers at $10K-15K/month. Every component must be built with multi-instance deployment in mind.

What must be instance-specific:

• Neo4j database (already scoped via `--instance-id`)

• NATS namespace (already scoped)

• Telegram bot token (per-client)

• Cloudflare Tunnel (per-client subdomain)

• Category ontology mappings (per-client, per-category)

• Knowledge seed data (per-client)

What should be shared across instances:

• Embedding model (all-mpnet-base-v2 — same model, different data)

• Claude CLI (OAuth — shared auth, isolated prompts)

• Station code (same codebase, different config)

• Dashboard framework (same UI, different data source)

• Appeal generator (same templates, different case history)

**Design constraint:** Every station must accept `instance_id` as a configuration parameter. No hardcoded paths, no shared databases, no cross-instance data leakage. The `customer-deploy.sh --instance-id` pattern already enforces this at Layer 0. Stations must honor it.

Perspective 5: What Could Go Wrong (Adversarial)

1. **Ontology drift.** Category mappings that work for horse equipment may not transfer to Fjallraven's outdoor gear. Each new category is a fresh mapping exercise ($2,500 each). If the mapping process isn't genuinely reusable, the "done once per category" promise becomes "done once per client per category."

2. **Knowledge leakage.** Carl wants exclusivity ("tailor-made"). If the codebase is the same across instances, what's actually exclusive? The answer must be: the ontology mappings, the category knowledge, the accumulated intelligence — not the code. KaaS monetizes process knowledge, not platform code.

3. **Exit valuation dependency.** 50/50 above $25M means Meridian's upside is tied to an acquisition that may never happen. The monthly retainer ($4,500) and profit share (20%) are the load-bearing revenue. Exit participation is optionality, not income.

4. **Scope expansion before proof.** Jennifer's wish list (PPC, account health, image QA) expands the scope beyond 8 stations. The $4,500/month covers "less than a strategist" — if scope grows, the unit economics break.

Phase 4: Blind Spot Analysis

1. Amazon as adversarial platform (not a reliable API)

The PDR treats Amazon's SP-API as a stable system. Amazon changes attribute requirements silently, suspends developer apps without warning, and inconsistently applies error codes per category. The rejection diagnostics parser (1A-8) is not a one-time build — it is a maintenance surface requiring continuous updates.

*Why invisible:* Engineering framing treats external APIs as stable contracts. Amazon treats its API as a tool it owns unilaterally.

*Damage:* Post-launch, 1A-8 becomes the highest-maintenance component with no maintenance budget. Competitive advantage erodes if categories change faster than ontology updates.

2. 55 calendar days assumes full Delphi utilization

The 363-hour estimate is correct for task hours. The 55-day calendar timeline assumes Delphi works on Rankona full-time. Delphi's primary assignment per boot-state is `#1850 Knowledge Extraction Marathon`. At 50% utilization: 110 calendar days. At 30%: 180+ calendar days.

*Why invisible:* Estimates are built from task decomposition, not from observed velocity or historical allocation data.

*Damage:* Carl may be expecting commercial results Q2 2026. Realistic timeline is Q3–Q4. Expectation must be set now.

3. "Measurable above baseline" means different things to each party

The profit share trigger is "measurable sales lift above baseline." But: the baseline is not captured. Amazon doesn't separate organic lift from listing optimization vs. advertising vs. seasonality. There is no defined mechanism for who calculates the baseline, using what data source, over what time window.

*Why invisible:* Commercial terms are assumed to be mutually understood. They are not — they're implicit.

*Damage:* At month 3, a 15% sales lift that Carl attributes to an ad campaign becomes a dispute about whether the 20% profit share applies.

4. The JV channel moat may undermine the Rankona moat

Category ontology "once mapped, every client in that category benefits." If JV channel onboards clients in horse equipment, they benefit from ontology mappings built with Rankona's data and Jennifer's expertise. Carl's "tailor-made" exclusivity is directly in tension with this.

*Why invisible:* JV channel is framed as future upside, not as a present tension with the exclusivity promise.

*Damage:* When the first JV client onboards in the same category, Rankona discovers their investment funded a competitor's playbook. Surfaces at Carl's next board meeting or renegotiation.

5. Cursor offload conflates delegation with work elimination

36% offload implies 36% reduction in Delphi hours. But cursor agents require Delphi to write specifications detailed enough to dispatch, review output for correctness, and validate integration with J components. Coordination overhead ≈ 20–30% of gross savings. Net savings: 20–25%.

*Why invisible:* Cursor offload is a planning construct. Management overhead is not in the estimate.

*Damage:* Delphi's available capacity is wrong by ~50 hours. The pressure relief valve is smaller than planned.
