WHAT WORKED

---

1. Michael's Warm Open

Severity: Preserve (High Value)

Michael opened with *"I know you've got a busy schedule, appreciate you making time."* Despite a 5-minute link failure and waiting period, rapport was re-established quickly. Acknowledging the prospect's time before making any ask sets a collaborative tone.

**Rule**: Always open by acknowledging the prospect's time explicitly. This is especially important after technical difficulties — it resets the energy without drawing attention to the failure.

---

2. Ely's Brick-Stacking Analogy

Severity: Preserve (High Value)

Ely explained agent specialization using a physical analogy — stacking bricks laterally vs. vertically, with each agent occupying a narrow, well-defined role. Non-technical operators can visualize it immediately.

> *"We started finding the engineering levers and dials to put in place in order to take the ones from the edges and start stacking them up so we could get higher performance in very specific ranges."*

**Improvement**: Anchor to prospect's industry. For Garrett: *"Imagine your agency had someone who only did ASIN optimization, someone who only read competitor pricing, and a manager who coordinated them but never touched the work — that's the structure."*

---

3. Alexander's Memory/Truncation Framing

Severity: Preserve (High Value)

The dementia framing for AI memory loss — *"these bots are coming back with dementia"* — was the most resonant product concept in the call. Garrett responded *"very interesting"* — one of only two moments of genuine engagement with the product.

**Rule**: Use this framing in the first 5 minutes of any product explanation. It creates a clear before/after: *"Every AI you've used has this problem. Here's what we built to solve it."* Do not follow it immediately with technical architecture — let the emotional resonance land.

---

4. Michael's Knowledge-Base-as-API Pivot

Severity: Preserve (Critical Value)

After Garrett said his agency wasn't the right fit, Michael improvised: *"What if we ingested your agency knowledge base and created a separate AI-powered entity with split equity? Your intellectual property becomes API calls."*

This found a genuine hook where none existed — asking someone in contraction mode to monetize what they already built, rather than asking them to grow.

**Develop**: Build a one-page leave-behind for this use case. Title: *"Your Expertise as Infrastructure."* Equity/revenue split model, ingestion process, sample use case.

---

5. Alexander's "Chum in the Water" Exit Hook

Severity: Preserve (Medium Value)

Rather than pressing for a hard close on the knowledge-base concept, Alexander planted the idea and let Garrett walk to his next meeting with it. No pressure, no follow-up ask — just a seed.

> *"We could take your intellectual property and turn that into a knowledge base for API calls... That might be the best case scenario."*

**Rule**: When a prospect signals mismatch or fatigue, leave a single compelling idea — don't close. The close belongs in the next contact.

---

6. Garrett Stayed 65+ Minutes

Severity: Data Point (Positive)

Garrett identified early that his agency wasn't the right fit but stayed engaged for over an hour. The product story is compelling even when the targeting is wrong. Fix the targeting and this retention becomes conversion.

**Impact**: The team had no contextual frame for Garrett's actual world. Every talking point, every discovery question, every reference point in the prep was wrong.

Severity: Critical

Alexander began pitching within minutes of Garrett finishing his background. The team never established what Garrett actually needed. The result: pitching a scaling tool to someone who doesn't want to scale.

> Garrett: *"I don't think my agency is the right fit because what we're downsizing and... I don't want to waste anyone's time."*

This is the direct output of pitching before listening.
