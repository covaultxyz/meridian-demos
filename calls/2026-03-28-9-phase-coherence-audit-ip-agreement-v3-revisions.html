PHASE 4: BLIND SPOT ANALYSIS

Blind Spot 1: The "Benefit to MBM" Framing Obscures Contractor Gains

**What is not being seen**: Every revision is framed exclusively as beneficial to MBM. The document never acknowledges that certain changes primarily serve Contractor interests. This framing is a negotiation posture, not an analytical output.

**Why the author's position makes it invisible**: The author is the Contractor. The document is designed to persuade MBM to accept the revisions. Acknowledging Contractor-primary benefits would weaken the negotiation position.

**Specific instances**:

• **ROFR narrowing (4.3)**: Primarily benefits Contractor by freeing the Partnership's addressable market. The "more enforceable" argument is true but secondary — MBM loses coverage of the broader healthcare domain.

• **Exhibit E mutual activation (10.E)**: Removes MBM's unilateral right to activate transfer pricing. Framed as "protecting MBM from untimely demands" when the original unilateral right was MBM's. MBM is the party losing optionality.

• **General Knowledge carve-out (2.4)**: Primarily protects Contractor's ability to reuse knowledge. The "MBM benefits from improved tools" argument assumes Contractor wouldn't improve tools without this clause, which is unproven.

• **Competitor termination right (10.7)**: Primarily protects Contractor from working for an acquirer they don't like. MBM keeps the IP, but loses the service relationship and institutional knowledge.

**Damage if unaddressed**: MBM's attorney may accept at face value that all revisions are MBM-favorable. If MBM later discovers they traded away ROFR breadth, Exhibit E control, and assignment flexibility based on this framing, trust in the relationship erodes.

Blind Spot 2: The Layered Architecture Model Privileges the Contractor's Classification

**What is not being seen**: The three-layer model (Infrastructure/Application/Data) creates a framework where Contractor's tools are categorized as "Infrastructure" — a term that connotes foundational necessity — while MBM's business logic is "Application" — a term that connotes dependency on the infrastructure below. This terminology subtly frames Contractor's IP as structurally essential.

**Why the author's position makes it invisible**: The author built the technology and naturally views the architecture from the builder's perspective. The layer names reflect how the Contractor thinks about the system.

**Damage if unaddressed**: In a future classification dispute, the layered model could be cited to argue that a component is "Infrastructure" (Contractor's) rather than "Application" (MBM's) based on its architectural position rather than who paid for its creation or who specified its requirements. The model creates a gravitational pull toward classifying borderline components as Contractor IP.

Blind Spot 3: The Document Assumes a Cooperative Relationship Will Persist

**What is not being seen**: Every dispute mechanism, update process, and classification procedure assumes good-faith cooperation. The document contains no provisions for adversarial scenarios beyond the IP dispute process (2.7). What happens when:

• Contractor stops updating Exhibit A (the 30-day requirement has no enforcement mechanism beyond dispute resolution)?

• One party uses the "operational continuity exception" (2.7(d)) as a de facto license to use disputed IP indefinitely?

• Contractor claims General Knowledge for implementation details that MBM considers Company IP?

**Why the author's position makes it invisible**: The author is currently in a cooperative relationship with MBM. Adversarial scenarios feel hypothetical and hostile to contemplate during negotiation.

**Damage if unaddressed**: The agreement's dispute-resolution mechanisms are calibrated for minor classification disagreements, not for fundamental relationship breakdown. The $50K binding threshold could be gamed by characterizing disputes as below the threshold when the actual commercial impact exceeds it.

Blind Spot 4: No Consideration of the "Bus Factor"

**What is not being seen**: The Contractor appears to be a small team (Mazzei/Bitler Partnership). If key personnel become unavailable (illness, dispute, competing priorities), MBM's ability to maintain, modify, or even deploy the Deliverables depends on institutional knowledge that may not be documented.

**Why the author's position makes it invisible**: Small teams rarely confront their own fragility in contractor agreements. The author assumes continuity of capability.

**Damage if unaddressed**: MBM owns the Company IP but may be unable to exercise that ownership practically. The perpetual license to Contractor Pre-Existing IP (Infrastructure Layer) is useless if MBM cannot find another contractor capable of working with MERIDIAN, MIG, or the Voice Pipeline — these are proprietary, non-commercial systems.

Blind Spot 5: Act 60 / Puerto Rico Jurisdictional Complexity Is Underexplored

**What is not being seen**: The document references Act 60 compliance (line 163) and Puerto Rico law (line 64) but does not address the jurisdictional complexity of a contractor agreement where one party is likely in Puerto Rico (Act 60 references suggest MBM operates under a PR decree) and the other may be elsewhere. Choice of law, venue, and tax implications of the IP ownership structure are material but not visible in these revisions.

**Why the author's position makes it invisible**: The author may view tax structure as outside the scope of IP revisions. But IP ownership, transfer pricing (Exhibit E), and Act 60 compliance are deeply intertwined.

**Damage if unaddressed**: If the IP ownership structure doesn't align with Act 60 decree requirements, MBM's tax benefits could be challenged by Hacienda. The transfer pricing framework (Exhibit E) exists precisely for this reason but is unactivated and now requires mutual consent.
