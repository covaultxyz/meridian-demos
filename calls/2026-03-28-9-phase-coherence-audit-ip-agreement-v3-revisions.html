PHASE 3: GAP ANALYSIS

CRITICAL Gaps

G1. Section 2.4 (No Retained Rights) is not revised — Contractor retains zero rights in Company IP

• **What's missing**: The original §2.4 states Contractor "shall retain no rights, licenses, claims, or interests of any kind in or to any Company IP" and cannot "use, reproduce, distribute, sublicense, or create derivative works based on Company IP for any purpose." This is absolute — Contractor cannot even reference past work in a portfolio or use learnings from the engagement.

• **Why it matters**: If the Application Layer (§3.6) includes custom configurations of Contractor's own tools, Contractor cannot improve those tools based on what they learned configuring them for MBM. This creates a knowledge trap where hands-on experience with your own platform becomes legally unusable.

• **Severity**: **CRITICAL** — This is the real risk vector, not the definitions. The General Knowledge carve-out (§3.2) partially addresses this but does not explicitly address the scenario where Contractor learns *how to improve their own tools* through MBM work.

G2. No liability cap anywhere in the agreement

• **What's missing**: Neither the original nor the revisions include a limitation of liability. Contractor's indemnification obligations (§8.1) are uncapped.

• **Why it matters**: A single IP infringement claim could expose Contractor to damages exceeding the entire contract value. Standard contractor agreements cap liability at 1-2x fees paid in the prior 12 months.

• **Severity**: **CRITICAL** — This is a standard enterprise contract term. Its absence is a significant negotiating oversight.

G3. Exhibit B (BAA) is still a placeholder

• **What's missing**: The HIPAA Phase 2 gate (§5.5 revision) correctly requires a BAA before PHI work begins, but Exhibit B remains "[To be attached separately]." No draft BAA exists.

• **Why it matters**: The gate is meaningless without the actual BAA. If MBM pushes to start Project 2 quickly, the pressure to execute a BAA without adequate review will be high. Having a draft ready gives Contractor leverage to ensure terms are reasonable.

• **Severity**: **CRITICAL** — The gate you created is correct but incomplete without the instrument it gates on.

HIGH Gaps

G4. Exhibit C (Compensation Schedule) is still a placeholder

• **What's missing**: Hourly rate, payment terms, expense policy, milestone structures are all blank.

• **Why it matters**: IP ownership is tied to compensation. If Contractor performs work and MBM disputes payment, the question of whether work product constitutes Company IP becomes entangled with unpaid compensation disputes. No payment = questionable consideration = questionable assignment.

• **Severity**: **HIGH** — Compensation is the consideration that makes the IP assignment enforceable.

G5. No revision to Section 10.7 (Assignment) — asymmetric

• **What's missing**: Original allows MBM to freely assign the agreement in connection with M&A, but Contractor requires MBM's prior written consent. No revision addresses this.

• **Why it matters**: If MBM is acquired by a larger healthcare company, all of Contractor's IP assignments and license grants transfer to the acquirer — potentially a competitor to the Partnership. Contractor has no consent right, no notification right, and no termination right upon assignment.

• **Severity**: **HIGH** — This is the classic "change of control" risk. Standard contractor protection includes a termination right or consent right upon assignment.

G6. No revision to Section 2.6 (Moral Rights Waiver)

• **What's missing**: Contractor irrevocably waives all moral rights (attribution, integrity, disclosure, withdrawal) in Company IP. This is not revised.

• **Why it matters**: In practice, moral rights waivers are common in US contracts (where moral rights are weak), but Puerto Rico follows a mixed common/civil law system. Under civil law traditions, moral rights are sometimes inalienable. The waiver may be unenforceable depending on the applicable IP law framework, and its presence without analysis creates a false sense of completeness.

• **Severity**: **HIGH** — Not because the waiver is harmful per se, but because its enforceability in PR jurisdiction is unanalyzed.

G7. No Statement of Work (SOW) is attached or referenced with specificity

• **What's missing**: The agreement references "applicable Statements of Work" (§1.4, §4.3 revised ROFR) but no SOW is attached. The revised ROFR specifically scopes to "the specific Project Deliverables described in the applicable Statement of Work."

• **Why it matters**: The narrowed ROFR's effectiveness depends entirely on how broadly or narrowly the SOW defines "Project Deliverables." Without a SOW, the ROFR has no anchor. MBM could draft a broad SOW later that re-expands the ROFR to near-original scope.

• **Severity**: **HIGH** — The ROFR revision's value is contingent on a document that doesn't exist yet.

MEDIUM Gaps

G8. No revision addresses §8.1 Indemnification asymmetry

• **What's missing**: Contractor indemnifies MBM for (a) breach of reps/warranties, (b) IP infringement of deliverables, (c) injury/death/property damage from negligence. MBM only indemnifies Contractor for IP infringement from MBM's independent use/commercialization of Company IP. The scope is asymmetric.

• **Why it matters**: Contractor bears broader indemnification risk. Combined with G2 (no liability cap), this is a significant exposure.

• **Severity**: **MEDIUM** (downgraded from HIGH because indemnification asymmetry is somewhat standard in contractor agreements, but the combination with no liability cap elevates risk)

G9. No revision addresses notice method (§10.6)

• **What's missing**: Notices must be in writing via certified mail or overnight courier. No provision for email or electronic notices.

• **Why it matters**: In a modern software development engagement with frequent IP classification decisions, requiring certified mail for every notice is impractical. The 15-day and 30-day windows in the new §2.7 dispute process will be difficult to exercise via certified mail.

• **Severity**: **MEDIUM** — Creates friction in the dispute resolution process the revisions themselves create.

G10. Item #6 in Exhibit A is dangerously vague

• **What's missing**: "General development frameworks, libraries, tools, and reusable code patterns used by Contractor across multiple clients and projects" — no specificity.

• **Why it matters**: This catch-all could be challenged by MBM as overly broad. It could also create classification disputes if Contractor claims a component is "general-purpose" and MBM claims it was developed for the Project.

• **Severity**: **MEDIUM** — The other 7 items are specific; this one undermines the specificity of the entire exhibit.

LOW Gaps

G11. No Exhibit D referenced

• **What's missing**: The agreement goes from Exhibit C to Exhibit E. No Exhibit D is defined or referenced.

• **Why it matters**: Minor formatting issue, but a hostile reader might wonder if an exhibit was intentionally omitted.

• **Severity**: **LOW**

G12. No revision addresses §6.5 payment timing

• **What's missing**: 30-day payment terms with no late-payment interest or penalty.

• **Why it matters**: Contractor has no leverage if MBM pays late. In a relationship where IP ownership is at stake, late payment creates a power imbalance.

• **Severity**: **LOW**

PHASE 4: BLIND SPOT ANALYSIS

BS1: Written from the Contractor's perspective — cannot see how MBM's attorney reads it

**What is not being seen**: The revisions are written as Contractor-side improvements. Every change benefits Contractor or creates bilateral obligations. No change benefits MBM or addresses MBM's legitimate concerns. A negotiation document that only takes from the counterparty and gives nothing triggers rejection instincts.

**Why the author's position makes it invisible**: The author is Contractor's advisor, optimizing for Contractor's protection. This is the correct role, but the document presents itself as "preserving MBM's absolute ownership" (a claim about neutrality) while actually being a Contractor advocacy document. The framing masks the advocacy.

**Damage if unaddressed**: MBM's attorney will immediately see this as a one-sided redline. The repeated framing of "these changes don't weaken Omar's position" will read as protesting too much. A more effective approach would acknowledge: "Here is what we're asking for, here is what MBM gains from each change, and here are the trade-offs."

BS2: Assumes the three-layer model is self-evident — it is not

**What is not being seen**: The Layered Architecture Acknowledgment (§3.6) assumes the reader understands what "Infrastructure Layer," "Application Layer," and "Data Layer" mean in the context of MERIDIAN's architecture. It doesn't define these layers by reference to specific technical components — only by abstract category.

**Why the author's position makes it invisible**: The author (and Contractor) built the system. They know exactly which component lives in which layer. Omar and MBM's attorney have never seen the architecture diagram. "Infrastructure Layer — Contractor Pre-Existing IP" is a conclusion, not a demonstration.

**Damage if unaddressed**: MBM's attorney will argue that the layer boundaries are arbitrary and self-serving. The classification of what's "independent of the Project" vs. "created specifically for MBM" becomes subjective without a technical appendix mapping actual components to layers.

BS3: Conflates enforceability with fairness

**What is not being seen**: The document claims changes make the agreement "more enforceable" (line 175). What the changes actually do is make the agreement more *balanced*. These are different properties. A perfectly one-sided agreement can be highly enforceable. A balanced agreement may be less enforceable if the additional terms create ambiguity or dispute opportunities.

**Why the author's position makes it invisible**: From Contractor's perspective, a balanced agreement feels more enforceable because Contractor would actually sign it and comply. But enforceability is a legal property, not a comfort property.

**Damage if unaddressed**: If this framing is presented to Omar, his attorney may point out (correctly) that the IP dispute fast-track and POA limitations actually reduce enforceability of MBM's IP rights by adding procedural hurdles. This undermines the credibility of the "more enforceable" framing.

BS4: Does not account for the relationship dynamics of presenting a counter-proposal

**What is not being seen**: This document was produced by AI analysis of a legal agreement. Presenting it as a formal counter-proposal could signal to Omar that Contractor is lawyering up, escalating the relationship, or treating the engagement as adversarial rather than collaborative.

**Why the author's position makes it invisible**: From a contract analysis perspective, identifying gaps and proposing fixes is responsible due diligence. From a relationship perspective, a 15-item counter-proposal to what Omar's attorney already reviewed (v3 POST-ATTORNEY REVIEW) could be perceived as questioning the attorney's competence.

**Damage if unaddressed**: Could damage the collaborative tone referenced in the Recitals ("the Parties intend to build a long-term, trusting, and mutually beneficial technological collaboration"). The presentation strategy matters as much as the substance.

BS5: The advisory notes at the bottom reveal Contractor's internal strategy

**What is not being seen**: Lines 179-204 contain internal advisory commentary (e.g., "you should review and add/remove as needed," "I included the major MERIDIAN systems but you know your tooling better than I do"). This is internal counsel to Contractor, not contract language.

**Why the author's position makes it invisible**: The author treats the document as a working draft for Contractor review. But if this document is accidentally shared with MBM as-is (or if the file is discovered in a shared repository), the advisory notes reveal the Contractor's negotiation strategy.

**Damage if unaddressed**: Reveals that (a) Contractor is using AI to review legal agreements, (b) the Exhibit A entries were generated by AI, not from Contractor's personal inventory, (c) the framing strategy ("none of these revisions weaken Omar's position") is a deliberate rhetorical choice, not an objective assessment. Any of these could undermine Contractor's negotiating position.
