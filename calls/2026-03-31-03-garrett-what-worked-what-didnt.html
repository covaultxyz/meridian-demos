Garrett Taylor Call — What Worked & What Didn't

_Tactical call execution analysis | 2026-03-27_

---

What Worked

1. Alex's Closing Pitch: Knowledge-as-a-Service

> "We could take your intellectual property and turn that into a knowledge base for API calls. Other companies that need to do this, you're getting paid off of the information that you cultivated. You're not running an agency. You're just selling all of those years and effort that you put in."

This was the single most effective moment. Garrett had deflected every other pitch angle, but this one got "let me ponder it." It works because it aligns with his actual situation: he has deep expertise, doesn't want to operate, and wants passive income. **This should be the lead-in for the follow-up call.**

2. Letting Garrett Talk First

The cheatsheet said "let him talk first — 5 minutes." It actually went 10 minutes, and those 10 minutes were gold. Garrett revealed:

• His actual background (IB/VC, not wealth management)

• His intentional downsizing posture

• His investment philosophy and check sizes

• His new venture (Zestify)

This information was more valuable than the (wrong) research report. The strategy was sound — the team just didn't adapt what they heard into the pitch that followed.

3. Ely's "Bricks Stacking" Metaphor

> "A LLM by its own baseline is like a bunch of bricks stacked laterally... we found the engineering levers and dials to take the ones from the edges and start stacking them up so we could get higher performance in very specific ranges."

Non-technical, visual, memorable. Garrett could follow this. When Ely uses analogies, the pitch works. When the team uses jargon (daemons, coherence, truncation, NATS), it doesn't.

4. Ely's "Exoskeleton" Framing

> "We've been thinking of it like an exoskeleton — we want to make it invisible."

Clean. One word that communicates the entire value prop. This should be a first-line talking point for non-technical audiences.

5. Michael's Personal Rapport

Michael's friendship with Garrett kept the call warm even when the pitch ran long. "He's a strong guy to play golf with, very down to earth" — the personal trust meant Garrett stayed engaged and gave genuine advice instead of polite dismissals. **This is the real asset Michael brings to these calls.**

6. Fundraise Advice Extraction

Despite compressed time, the team got actionable fundraise guidance:

• SAFE or convertible note (don't set valuation now)

• 10% ESOP carve-out

• Act 60 structure: Delaware C-Corp + PR export services + transfer price study

• Friends & family first, then bridge to institutional

• His own precedent: $2M at $5M pre-money via SAFE

This is concrete, experienced advice from someone who's done it. Worth more than most paid consultations.

7. Holdback Discipline

The cheatsheet flagged 5 holdback items (Commerce Machine, Renatus/SPAC, specific pipeline names, burn rate, fundraising numbers). The team never surfaced any of them. Even though the research was wrong, the discipline around what NOT to say was perfect.

8. Ely's Deployment Architecture Pitch

> "Each deployment is essentially standalone... we built an incredibly high-functioning architecture and then we take and deploy that in a specific instance that would be customer-specific... there's a total air gap between one customer and another."

This addressed Garrett's question about customization directly and clearly. It's the right framing for enterprise buyers: isolated, secure, customer-specific.

What Didn't Work

1. Wrong Research Profile (Critical)

The entire prep package was built for a different person. The cheatsheet cards ("$3.7B AUM", "Forbes #40", "M&A specialist") were useless. The discovery questions were calibrated for a wealth management audience. The talking points referenced "seven thousand clients." None of this applied.

**Impact**: The team had to improvise the entire call. The beautiful prep package sat unused.

2. The 25-Minute Monologue

The cheatsheet said "Present MERIDIAN capabilities (15 min)." Three speakers took 25 minutes with no pause for Garrett to react, ask questions, or redirect. Key moments where Garrett was talked past:

• **Minute ~20**: Garrett says "It's very interesting. Thank you for sharing that." — This is a polite signal to move on. Instead, Michael jumped in with more voice agent details.

• **Minute ~25**: Ely starts explaining ontology. Garrett is listening but not engaging.

• **Minute ~30**: Finally, Garrett asks a question ("What level of customization?") — this should have happened at minute 15.

**Rule of thumb**: If the prospect hasn't asked a question in 5 minutes, you've lost them. Pause and ask "What questions does this raise for you?"

3. Technical Jargon Overload

The cheatsheet warned: "Avoid technical details about NATS, Neo4j, systemd, daemons." The call included:

• "Truncation" and "context windows" (Alex)

• "Coherence and dissonance frequencies" (Alex)

• "Prompt injection" (Alex)

• "25 terminal windows... hacker movies" (Alex)

• "Ontology" — Ely caught himself and explained it, but the word itself creates distance

• "Daemons" — used repeatedly without clear context for a non-technical listener

• "Orchestrator" (Alex/Ely)

• "API calls" (multiple speakers)

Garrett's stated AI experience: "ChatGPT and Claude, dabbled in Gemini." He's not technical. Every jargon word was friction.

4. Three Speakers, No Conductor

Alex pitched. Ely pitched. Michael pitched. Sometimes they overlapped:

• Michael: "Can you explain the suite of agents?" — directed at Ely

• Alex (later): "So another thing..." — interrupted Ely's flow

• Michael: "And here's the beauty..." — jumped in during Garrett's question

No one was clearly leading the call. The cheatsheet assigned Michael as the opener and Alexander as the presenter, but in practice all three were freelancing.

**Fix**: Designate one primary presenter per section. Others contribute only when directly asked or when they have a specific 30-second point.

5. Pushing the Wrong Use Case Three Times

Garrett said at minute 10: "I've been intentionally downsizing."

Garrett said at minute 40: "Is it commercially deployable?" (assessing the company, not buying)

Garrett said at minute 60: "My agency isn't the right test."

The team asked variations of "can we deploy this for your agency?" three times after he'd already said no. Each attempt made the pitch feel more desperate rather than more tailored.

**The right move after his first "no"**: "Totally understand — the agency isn't the fit. Let's flip this: given what you've seen of the platform, what kind of business would be the ideal deployment?"

6. No Demo Shown

The team had a working demo, a 5-act demo script, and a pre-flight checklist. None of it was used. The entire call was verbal. For someone who isn't technical and can't visualize daemons and knowledge graphs from description alone, showing > telling.

Even a 3-minute screen share of the dashboard or a Telegram interaction would have been more convincing than 25 minutes of description.

7. "What If You Had a Magic Wand?" — Too Open-Ended

Alex asked this twice. Both times Garrett didn't know what to do with it. The question works when someone has an expressed pain point and you're asking them to dream bigger. Garrett's pain point is the opposite — he wants to do LESS, not more.

A better version for this prospect: "If you could package what you know and get paid for it without running the agency, what would that look like?"

8. Didn't Identify His Hard Stop Early Enough

Garrett mentioned at minute ~72 that he had a call at the top of the hour. The fundraise discussion was already rushed by then. If the team had asked about timing at the start ("How much time do you have today?"), they could have compressed the pitch and expanded the advice section.
