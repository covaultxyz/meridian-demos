Meridian Master Services Agreement — Simple Breakdown

What Is This Document?

This is a **business agreement** — the rules for how Meridian works with a client. Think of it like the menu and house rules at a restaurant: it tells you what you can order, what it costs, and what you're allowed to do with what you buy.

The two groups are:

• **Meridian** — A technology company that builds AI-powered software systems. Meridian has its own tools, platforms, and technology that it built over years of work.

• **The Client** — A business that wants Meridian to build something for them. They're hiring Meridian for specific projects.

**The big question this agreement answers:** What does the Client actually get when they hire Meridian — and what stays with Meridian?

---

The Big Idea: "You're Buying a Meal, Not the Restaurant"

When you go to a restaurant, you pay for a meal. You get to eat it, enjoy it, take the leftovers home. But you don't own the kitchen, the recipes, the chef's skills, or the restaurant itself. If you want to open your own restaurant using their recipes, that's a completely different conversation — and a completely different price.

This agreement works the same way.

Meridian builds custom software for clients. The client gets what they ordered (the meal). But Meridian's tools, platform, code libraries, and technology (the kitchen, the recipes, the skills) stay with Meridian. If a client wants to actually own part of Meridian's technology, they can — but that's a separate deal with a separate price tag.

**Why this matters:** The previous version of this contract started from the assumption that the client owns everything Meridian builds. That's like assuming the customer owns the restaurant because they paid for dinner. This agreement fixes that by starting from the right place: Meridian owns its technology, and the client gets clearly defined rights.

---

Article 1 — Purpose: What This Agreement Is (and Isn't)

What It IS

• A set of rules for how Meridian provides services to the client

• A clear statement that Meridian owns its technology

• A way to define exactly what the client gets for their money

What It Is NOT

• **Not an IP giveaway** — Meridian doesn't hand over its core technology

• **Not a work-for-hire arrangement** — Meridian is a technology company, not an employee

• **Not a buyout** — buying Meridian's platform requires a completely separate deal

• **Not a blanket transfer** — nothing passes to the client automatically just because they paid for services

The Golden Rule

> Just because a client pays for work doesn't mean they own Meridian's tools, recipes, or kitchen. Payment buys services and defined deliverables — not Meridian's underlying technology.

---

Article 2 — Who Owns What

Meridian Background IP — "The Kitchen and the Recipes"

Everything Meridian built before working with the client — and everything Meridian builds independently — belongs to Meridian. This includes:

• The software platform

• Code libraries and frameworks

• Deployment tools and infrastructure

• AI models and orchestration systems

• Security tooling

• General methods and know-how

• Any improvements to the above, even if inspired by client work

Think of it as the restaurant's kitchen, equipment, recipes, and cooking techniques. The client never owns these, no matter how many meals they order.

Client Deliverables — "The Meal You Ordered"

The specific things Meridian builds *exclusively* for the client under a signed project plan. These are the custom items — like a meal made to your exact specifications.

Client Deliverables might include:

• Custom patient intake workflows

• Practice-specific business rules

• User interfaces designed to the client's requirements

• Client-specific application logic

Client Deliverables do **NOT** include:

• Meridian's platform or frameworks

• Reusable code or modules

• Deployment tools

• Anything Meridian uses for other clients too

How To Tell the Difference

| Question | If Yes → | If No → |

|----------|----------|---------|

| Was it built *exclusively* for this client? | Might be a Client Deliverable | Meridian Background IP |

| Is it listed in the signed project plan (SOW)? | Client Deliverable | Meridian Background IP |

| Could Meridian use it for another client? | Meridian Background IP | Might be a Client Deliverable |

| Did it exist before this project started? | Meridian Background IP | Check the other questions |

No Sneaky Transfers

Nothing transfers to the client automatically. Not by:

• Paying a bill

• Working together on a project

• Giving Meridian access to client systems

• Letting Meridian use client data

• Embedding Meridian tools in the client's software

If the client wants to own something, it has to be written down specifically in a signed document.

Improvements Stay with Meridian

If Meridian makes its platform better while working on a client project, those improvements belong to Meridian. It's like if a chef invents a new sauce while cooking your dinner — the sauce is theirs, even though your meal inspired it.

Work Before the Project Plan? Meridian's.

If someone starts work before the official project plan is signed, everything created belongs to Meridian by default. The parties can agree to change that later, but the starting position is clear: no plan = Meridian's property.

Article 3 — The License Menu (What You Can Buy)

This is the heart of the new agreement. Instead of one option ("you own everything"), there are now **four levels** of rights a client can buy. Think of it like membership tiers:

Tier 1 — Standard License (Included in Base Price)

**What you get**: Use the software Meridian built for you, inside your own business.

**What you can do**:

• Run the software for your own operations

• Have your own team use it

**What you can't do**:

• Share it with other companies

• Resell it

• Modify Meridian's underlying tools

• Let partners or subsidiaries use it

**Analogy**: You bought a meal. You can eat it. You can't serve it to other people's tables.

Tier 2 — Extended License (Premium)

**What you get**: Everything in Tier 1, plus more flexibility.

**What you can do**:

• Let your affiliated companies use it

• Hire a different tech company to maintain or modify it

• Move it to different servers or platforms

**What you can't do**:

• Resell it to unrelated third parties

• White-label it as your own product

• Sublicense it

**Analogy**: You bought a meal AND you can take it home, reheat it, share it with your family, and take it to a different kitchen if you want. But you can't open a food truck with it.

Tier 3 — Enterprise License (Major Premium)

**What you get**: Everything in Tier 2, plus commercial flexibility.

**What you can do**:

• Sublicense to your own customers as part of your services

• Create new versions and variations

• Transfer the license if your company gets acquired

**What you can't do**:

• Sell Meridian's platform or tools as standalone products

• Claim Meridian's technology as your own

**Analogy**: You can now cater events with the meal, customize it for different customers, and even sell your own version. But you can't claim you invented the recipe or sell the restaurant's kitchen equipment.

Tier 4 — Buyout (Separate Agreement, Separate Price)

**What you get**: Actual ownership of specifically identified items.

**How it works**:

• Requires a completely separate agreement (Exhibit G)

• Requires its own price negotiation — this isn't included in service fees

• Only covers the exact items listed in the buyout agreement

• Meridian keeps the right to use the technology too (unless they agree to give that up)

• Does NOT automatically include Meridian's platform or Background IP

**Analogy**: You're buying the recipe itself, not just the meal. That's a different transaction with a different price. And even then, the chef can still cook with those techniques for other people unless you pay extra for exclusivity.

Why This Is Better Than Before

| Before (v3-v6) | Now (MSLA) |

|----------------|------------|

| Client automatically owns everything built for them | Client gets what they pay for — nothing more, nothing less |

| Meridian had to prove what it could keep | Client's rights are defined by the tier they buy |

| One option: give away the IP | Four options: use it, share it, commercialize it, or buy it |

| Price = hourly rate only | Price = hourly rate + the level of rights you want |

Article 4 — The Money Part

You Need a Plan Before Work Starts

Before Meridian does anything, both sides sign a **Statement of Work (SOW)** — a written plan that covers:

• What's being built

• How much it costs

• When payment is due

• What the client gets (which license tier)

• Whether patient data is involved

• How to test and approve the work

No plan = no work. This protects both sides.

Deposits

The client pays a deposit before work begins — typically 50% of the estimated project cost, or the first milestone payment. Think of it like paying a deposit for a catering order.

Getting Paid

• **Monthly billing** based on hours worked, or milestone-based — whatever the SOW says

• **Net 30** — client has 30 days to pay each invoice

• **Late fees**: 1.5% per month (like a credit card late fee)

• **Disputed bills**: Client has 15 days to flag problems, but still has to pay the parts they don't dispute

• **Paying a bill doesn't mean approving the work** — those are separate things

Rate Schedule

| What | Hourly Rate |

|------|-------------|

| Regular coding work | $165/hour |

| Design and architecture | $200/hour |

| Expert consulting | $275/hour |

Article 5 — Testing and Warranties

Checking the Work

The client gets **20 business days** to review each deliverable. For big releases (platform launches, security-sensitive features, anything over 100 hours of work), the client has to actually sign off — it's not considered accepted just because they didn't say no.

If problems are found, Meridian fixes them within 10 business days at no extra charge.

The Warranty — "If It Breaks, We Fix It"

For **90 days** after the client accepts a deliverable, Meridian fixes for free:

• Bugs and defects

• Hidden problems that didn't show up during testing

• Security holes in Meridian's code

• Things that don't work the way the plan said they should

If Meridian fixes a bug, the warranty restarts for 30 days on that specific fix — so you're not stuck with a bad repair.

After the Warranty

Ongoing support after the 90-day warranty is a separate arrangement. It's like a car warranty — the manufacturer covers problems for a set period, and after that you can buy an extended service plan.

---

Article 6 — Keeping Secrets and Protecting Patient Data

Secrets Stay Secret

Both sides agree to keep each other's confidential information private. Standard stuff.

Patient Data

Client data always belongs to the client. If a project involves patient health information (PHI), a special **HIPAA agreement (BAA)** must be signed before that work starts. No exceptions.

Every project plan (SOW) has to state upfront whether patient data is involved — no discovering it halfway through.

Security

Both sides promise to keep their systems secure. If either side gets hacked or has a security problem, they tell the other within 72 hours.

---

Article 7 — Promises Both Sides Make

• **Meridian promises**: The work will be professional quality, it won't steal anyone else's technology, and Meridian actually owns what it claims to own

• **The Client promises**: The data they provide is lawfully collected, and they won't use the software illegally

• **Both sides promise**: They're following Puerto Rico's tax rules (Act 60) and have proper insurance

---

Article 8 — What Happens If Something Goes Wrong

If there's a regular business mistake (not stealing or fraud), the most either side can owe is **2 times what the client paid in the last year, or $250,000** — whichever is more.

**But there's no limit at all for**:

• Stealing intellectual property

• Leaking patient data

• Leaking secrets

• Fraud or intentional harm

• Data breach cleanup costs

• Court orders

---

Article 9 — Starting, Ending, and Handing Things Over

The Contract Lasts Until Someone Ends It

Either side can end the agreement with 60 days' notice. If someone breaks the rules and doesn't fix it within 30 days, the other side can end it immediately.

What Happens When It Ends

1. **Licenses the client already paid for continue** — you don't lose what you bought

2. **Meridian delivers all Client Deliverables** — source code, documentation, everything the client owns

3. **Meridian helps with the transition** — 90 days of handover support, at least 40 hours of training, cooperation with any replacement

4. **Meridian's technology stays with Meridian** — the platform, tools, and Background IP don't go with the client

The Client Gets Keys During the Whole Project

While the project is running (not just at the end), Meridian gives the client access to:

• The source code for their deliverables

• Build instructions and deployment guides

• Login credentials for the client's own systems

• A list of every tool and outside service the software depends on

This means if Meridian disappears tomorrow, the client can still keep their software running.

If the Client Gets Bought By a Competitor

If the client sells their business to a company that competes with Meridian, Meridian can start a 6-month exit process. During those 6 months, Meridian keeps working, provides training, and cooperates with the replacement. All licenses the client already paid for survive.

The Bottom Line

1. **Meridian owns its technology.** The platform, tools, frameworks, libraries, and know-how belong to Meridian — always.

2. **The client gets what they pay for — no more, no less.** Specific deliverables built exclusively for the client, under a signed project plan, at an agreed price.

3. **Rights come in four tiers.** Basic use is included. Broader use (affiliates, migration, resale, sublicensing) costs more. Actual ownership is a separate deal.

4. **Nothing transfers automatically.** Not by paying bills, not by working together, not by integration, not by access. Only by signed, specific written agreement.

5. **A project plan is required before any work starts.** No plan, no work. Work done before a plan is signed belongs to Meridian.

6. **Improvements stay with Meridian.** If Meridian's tools get better during a client project, those improvements belong to Meridian.

7. **Patient data is always the client's.** And it's protected by special rules (HIPAA/BAA) that must be in place before any patient data work begins.

8. **The client gets keys to their deliverables from day one.** Source code, documentation, and build instructions are shared throughout the project, not just at the end.

9. **If it breaks, Meridian fixes it for 90 days.** Including hidden problems, security holes, and things that don't match the specifications.

10. **If the client wants to buy Meridian's technology, that's a separate conversation.** With a separate price. A licensing fee or a buyout — not an assumption baked into the services contract.
