2.6 Moral Rights Waiver (revised — v6)

To the fullest extent permitted by applicable law, Contractor irrevocably waives and agrees never to assert any and all moral rights (including rights of attribution, integrity, disclosure, and withdrawal) that Contractor may have in any Company IP. The Parties acknowledge that Puerto Rico Act No. 55 of March 9, 2012 (Ley de Derechos Morales de Autor de Puerto Rico), which replaced Act No. 96 of July 15, 1988, recognizes moral rights (derechos morales) — including attribution, integrity, disclosure, and withdrawal — as "irrenunciable e inalienables" (non-waivable and inalienable) personal rights of the author. To the extent any moral right is deemed inalienable under Act 55-2012, the Puerto Rico Civil Code (Title 31, Chapter 163, §§ 1401ff-1402m), or applicable federal law (including VARA, 17 U.S.C. § 106A), Contractor agrees not to exercise such right in a manner that would interfere with MBM's exercise of its rights under this Agreement, including but not limited to MBM's rights to modify, commercialize, sublicense, create derivative works of, anonymize, and reuse Company IP in any manner consistent with Section 2.3. > **What changed**: Updated to cite Act 55 of 2012 (current governing statute, replacing Act 96 of 1988). The revision uses: (1) a targeted partial integrity waiver for MBM's modifications to Company IP, and (2) a non-exercise covenant for attribution/disclosure/withdrawal (which cannot be waived). Supported by *Pancorbo v. Wometco de Puerto Rico, Inc.*, 115 D.P.R. 495 (1984) and *Gener-Villar v. Adcom Group, Inc.*, 417 F.3d 201 (1st Cir. 2005).

> **What changed (v6 — NH-2)**: Broadened the non-exercise covenant scope from generic "interfere with MBM's exercise of its rights" to explicitly enumerate: modify, commercialize, sublicense, create derivative works of, anonymize, and reuse Company IP consistent with Section 2.3.

> **Benefit to MBM**: Maximum protection available under PR law — a partial integrity waiver plus a non-exercise covenant with enumerated scope. This is stronger than the original blanket waiver, which a PR court would likely strike entirely under Act 55-2012, leaving MBM with zero protection. This revision is drafted to survive judicial scrutiny. The enumerated non-exercise covenant scope provides clear precedent for MBM's full exploitation rights.

> **Benefit to Contractor**: Addresses the real legal risk that the original blanket waiver is unenforceable under PR law. The partial waiver approach gives MBM durable protection while respecting the statutory framework Contractor operates under.

2.7 IP Classification Disputes (revised — v6)

In the event of a dispute regarding whether specific intellectual property constitutes Company IP, Contractor Pre-Existing IP, Hybrid Component, or General Knowledge, the Parties shall:

(a) First attempt to resolve the dispute through good faith discussion within fifteen (15) business days of either Party raising the dispute in writing (which may be delivered by email pursuant to Section 10.6);

(b) If unresolved, engage an independent intellectual property attorney, mutually agreed upon by both Parties. If the Parties cannot agree within ten (10) business days, each Party shall nominate one candidate and the two nominees shall jointly select the independent attorney. If the nominees cannot agree within five (5) additional business days, either Party may request JAMS (or its successor) to appoint the independent attorney;

(c) The independent attorney's recommendation shall be binding on both Parties for disputes where the estimated commercial value of the disputed IP is less than $50,000. For purposes of this Section 2.7(c), "estimated commercial value" shall be determined by the independent attorney as part of the classification recommendation, using the fair market value of the disputed IP as of the date of the dispute notice, based on (i) the cost of development, (ii) comparable licensing or assignment transactions, or (iii) discounted future revenue attributable to the disputed IP, whichever methodology the independent attorney determines to be most appropriate. For disputes where the estimated commercial value equals or exceeds $50,000, either Party may initiate formal dispute resolution under Section 10.1 within fifteen (15) business days of receiving the recommendation; the Party that initiates formal dispute resolution after a recommendation has been issued shall bear the other Party's reasonable costs and attorneys' fees if the formal resolution reaches the same conclusion as the independent attorney's recommendation;

(d) During the pendency of any IP classification dispute, neither Party shall use, license, transfer, or encumber the disputed IP without the other Party's written consent, except that each Party may continue to use the disputed IP solely to the extent necessary to maintain ongoing operations and Deliverable functionality without interruption;

(e) The cost of the independent attorney shall be shared equally between the Parties, except as provided in subsection (c) above.

The burden of proof for rebutting this presumption shall rest with the Party claiming the item is not Company IP.

(g) **Title Preservation During Disputes.** Notwithstanding subsection (d), MBM's rights in Company IP (including the right to represent ownership in financing, diligence, licensing, and enforcement proceedings) shall not be affected by the pendency of any IP classification dispute, except with respect to the specific items expressly identified in the dispute notice. A pending dispute regarding one component shall not cloud MBM's title to any other Company IP.

> **What changed (v5)**: New fast-track dispute process. v5 improvements over v2: (1) JAMS fallback for attorney selection deadlock (REC-4 from Bacherman Martin review); (2) defined methodology for "estimated commercial value" — eliminates the incentive to inflate value to avoid binding recommendation; (3) email-eligible notice; (4) cost-shifting for frivolous escalations; (5) operational continuity exception.

> **What changed (v6 — MF-3)**: Added subsection (f) establishing a rebuttable presumption that all SOW work product is Company IP unless specifically identified in Exhibit A with module-level specificity and pre-dating the engagement. Added subsection (g) preserving MBM's title to non-disputed IP during any pending dispute — eliminates the "title cloud" risk in diligence and financing.

The defined valuation methodology prevents gaming. Burden of proof is now on the party claiming something is NOT Company IP. 9. **Affirmative PHI classification** (MF-7) — determination before work starts, not mid-stream
